🌟 Embracing Change

Advocating for Your Worth and Exploring New Opportunities

📍 The Situation: Feeling Undervalued and Stuck

"If you want a better life, change has to happen."

What You're Experiencing

Emotionally: Significant sadness, sense of stagnation

At work: Feeling undervalued and underpaid despite long tenure and contributions

Your desire: A "reset" — to "get back" to a better emotional and professional place

The Tension You're Navigating

On one hand: Strong desire for change and new opportunities

On the other hand: Hesitant due to uncertainty and comfort of current situation

The comfort includes: Some accommodations for your needs at current job

What You're Considering

  • Seeking new employment
  • Meeting with employer to discuss your value
  • Possibly negotiating for better compensation or new role
The core problem: Ongoing dissatisfaction stemming from being undervalued at work and feeling stuck.

The deeper issue: Your current life and job were shaped more by necessity than by intentional choice.

The mismatch: Your actual worth vs. how you're treated or compensated.

The pattern identified: Resistance to change keeps you from seeking or receiving what you truly deserve.

🗺️ The Change and Advocacy Framework

"The life that you have now wasn't created for you, by you intentionally. It was created by your needs, for your needs."

The Seven-Step Process

1 Recognize the Need for Change

The truth: A better life requires intentional change, not just adaptation to circumstances

Your signal: The sadness and frustration you're feeling aren't just passing moods — they're signals that your current situation isn't serving you

The therapist's insight: "The life that you have now wasn't created for you, by you intentionally. It was created by your needs, for your needs."

Why this matters: Recognizing the need for change breaks the cycle of stagnation

2 Assess Your Current Value and Contributions

What to do: Objectively list your achievements and the value you bring to your workplace

Include:

  • Major projects you've completed
  • Achievements and positive feedback
  • Quantified results (revenue generated, problems solved)
  • Unique skills and perspectives you bring

The therapist's reminder: "You need to have your value because you're a Black woman. And you need evidence."

Why this matters: Assessing value builds confidence and provides concrete material for negotiation

3 Explore External Opportunities

What to do: Actively look for other jobs

The purpose is NOT just about leaving:

  • Understand your market worth
  • See what's available
  • Get perspective on your current situation
  • Gain leverage for negotiations

The therapist's guidance: "You don't have to take anything. But being aware — being in the know — will be extremely helpful."

"Looking at it is one thing, but experiencing it is another."

Remember: "As much as they are interviewing you, you are interviewing them."

Why this matters: Exploring opportunities gives perspective and leverage; experiencing interviews is more powerful than just considering them

4 Prepare for Negotiation

What to do: Gather evidence of your value and be ready to present it to your employer

Organize:

  • Your documented achievements
  • Market research on salary ranges
  • Notes from job applications or interviews
  • What you want to say, focusing on your value

The therapist's advice: Do your due diligence — the necessary preparation and research before making a decision

Why this matters: Preparation ensures you advocate effectively

5 Advocate for Yourself

What to do: Communicate your worth directly, focusing on your unique contributions

DO focus on:

  • Your specific achievements and value
  • What you uniquely bring to the organization
  • Concrete evidence of your contributions
  • Your own experience and perspective

DON'T:

  • Make threats (unless you mean it)
  • Compare yourself to others
  • Speak for others ("people are concerned") unless necessary

The therapist's guidance: "If they cannot see that after you show them all the things, how valuable you are, then you can let them learn."

Why this matters: Self-advocacy is essential for self-respect and fair treatment, especially for Black women in the workplace

6 Accept Uncertainty and Prepare for Multiple Outcomes

The truth: Change can be risky but also rewarding

Be ready for:

  • Your employer accepting your request
  • Your employer rejecting your request
  • A new job working out well
  • A new job not being the right fit

The therapist's reminder: "Prepare either way."

The important truth: It's normal to feel uncertain about change, but the risk can bring rewards

Why this matters: Accepting uncertainty reduces fear of failure

7 Continue Seeking and Learning

What to do: Even if negotiations don't yield results, keep exploring and applying elsewhere

If a new job doesn't work out: You can always keep searching — don't settle

The therapist's insight: "The resistance to change keeps you in a space where you don't get what you deserve."

Why this matters: Ongoing search prevents complacency and opens new doors

Important Reminder About Accommodations

Reasonable accommodations: Adjustments at work to support employees with specific needs

Key truth: You can request these in ANY job, not just your current one

Don't let this hold you back: Your accommodations aren't unique to your current employer

💪 Special Considerations: Advocating as a Black Woman

"You need to have your value because you're a Black woman. And you need evidence."

Why Self-Advocacy Is Especially Important

The reality: Black women are often overlooked and undervalued in the workplace

What this means for you:

  • You need documented evidence of your value
  • You need to advocate clearly and directly
  • You deserve fair compensation and respect
  • Self-advocacy is not just important — it's essential

The Salary Reality Check

The therapist's observation: "You always overestimate [your pay], especially when you think about what they actually pay, but also what you are actually worth."

What this means:

  • You likely think you're paid more than you actually are
  • You're probably worth more than you're currently being paid
  • Overestimate your salary expectations to account for undervaluation
