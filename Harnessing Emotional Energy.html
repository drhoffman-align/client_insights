Therapeutic Insights

Alignment Psychiatry

The Situation

You are navigating a period of transition focused on establishing consistent routines, maintaining organization, and improving self-care. You struggle with boundaries, often prioritizing others' needs over your own, and experience avoidance around tasks triggering fears of rejection or inadequacy. Your sleep schedule has been inconsistent, and you're optimizing your ADHD medication routine while engaging in personal development to address underlying insecurities.

"Emotions are information, not facts—use them as signals, not instructions."

Core Problem

You allow anxiety and avoidance to dictate your actions, particularly when facing tasks that could lead to rejection or challenge your self-concept. This results in procrastination, overcommitting to others, difficulty maintaining routines, and a mismatch between your actual capabilities and self-perception fueled by deeply held insecurities about hierarchy and self-worth.

Clinical Concepts

Core Beliefs
Deeply held assumptions about oneself, others, and the world, often formed in childhood
Automatic Thoughts
Immediate, often unconscious thoughts (images, judgments, assumptions) arising in response to situations
Fight/Flight/Freeze/Fawn
The body's automatic threat responses; you tend toward fawning (people-pleasing) and freezing (avoidance)
Emotional Energy
Emotions fuel or drain capacity to act; positive emotions energize, negative emotions can deplete
Valid vs. True Emotions
Feelings are always valid (real to you) but not always true (accurate reflections of reality)
"Your value is not determined by others' perceptions."

Therapeutic Frameworks

CBT Model: Think-Feel-Do
1
Core beliefs shape our "bubble of beliefs"
2
Thoughts arise automatically from those beliefs
3
Emotions are generated by thoughts
4

Emotional Energy Management
1
Recognize emotions as energy
2
Identify the source
3
Transform the energy into purposeful action
4
Practice insight through reflection
Key Insight: Avoidance masks deeper fears of rejection or inadequacy. By processing these emotions intentionally, you can transform anxious energy into concrete actions rather than letting it fuel procrastination.

Practical Instructions

Daily Emotional Processing: Each morning during your personal development time, write down one fear or anxiety you're experiencing. Process it using the five W's and H (Who, What, When, Where, Why, How). Identify whether the belief is true and determine a concrete action or reframe.

Time Blocking with Boundaries: Set specific times for helping others. This protects your energy and ensures you have time for your own priorities.

Morning Preparation: Place your medications and caffeine by your bed the night before. This simple step supports consistent timing and removes barriers to your morning routine.

Homework

  • Daily emotional processing: Write down one fear/anxiety each morning during personal development time
  • Process each using five W's and H (Who, What, When, Where, Why, How)
  • Identify if the belief is true and determine concrete action or reframe
  • Bring written reflections to next session
  • Time block with boundaries: Set specific times for helping others
  • Morning prep: Place meds and caffeine by bed the night before
"Fawning and people-pleasing are learned responses that can be unlearned."

Key Takeaways

  • Emotions are information, not facts—use them as signals, not instructions
  • Core beliefs shape automatic thoughts and emotional responses
  • Anxiety can be harnessed as energy for action through intentional processing
  • Avoidance masks deeper fears of rejection or inadequacy
  • Setting time boundaries is essential for self-care and productivity
  • Consistent routines conserve mental energy
  • Challenge fears with evidence—not all fears are true
  • Your value is not determined by others' perceptions
  • Fawning and people-pleasing are learned responses that can be unlearned
  • Reflecting after emotional events builds insight and resilience
  • Channel anxious energy into concrete actions, not avoidance
  • Medication works best with consistent timing and preparation
Bottom Line: You have the capacity to transform anxiety from a barrier into a tool for growth. By processing your emotions intentionally, challenging the accuracy of your fears, and establishing consistent routines with clear boundaries, you can break free from avoidance patterns and build a life aligned with your true capabilities—not the limiting beliefs formed in your past. Your worth is inherent, not earned through others' approval.
