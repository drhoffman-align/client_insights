🌐 Framework #1: Delegation and Network Mapping

"If it's only you, then what happens if you're not there? The show goes on."

The Five-Step Process

1 List All Ongoing Projects and Tasks

What to do: Write down everything you're currently responsible for in both work and business

Be comprehensive: Include both major projects and recurring tasks

2 Identify Which Tasks Only You Can Do

What to do: Review your list and mark tasks that truly require your unique skills or knowledge with a star

Ask yourself: "Could someone else do this if properly trained?"

3 For Starred Tasks, Brainstorm Ways to Involve Others

What to do: Even for tasks only you can do now, think about how to train others or break them into teachable parts

Ask: "If I'm not here, who could do this? How can I train them?"

What delegation is: Sharing tasks with others so you're not the only point of failure. In your life, this means teaching others about your business processes.

4 Create a "Network Tree" or Web Diagram

What to do: Draw a visual map showing who is responsible for what, or who could be

Include:

  • Your name at the center
  • Tasks/responsibilities branching out
  • Names of people who could handle each task
  • Training needed for each delegation

5 Regularly Review and Update the Map

What to do: As roles and tasks shift, update your network tree monthly

Why: Keeps delegation current and identifies new bottlenecks before they become problems

Why this framework matters:

Delegation prevents bottlenecks — Work doesn't stop when you're unavailable

Reduces stress — You're not the single point of failure

Ensures continuity — Business runs smoothly even in your absence

Prepares you for higher-level roles — Teaching and standard-setting are key leadership skills

How this applies to you: You currently handle most business tasks yourself. By mapping out responsibilities and training others, you can reduce your load and ensure the business runs smoothly even in your absence.

The Metaphor

"If something happens to you, the show goes on"

The business and life should not depend solely on one person.

📅 Framework #2: Time-Blocking and Routine Structuring

"Set strict boundaries so you're giving all the time you need to all the things."

The Four-Step Process

1 Break Your Day into Blocks

What to include:

  • Work
  • Business
  • Fitness
  • Personal development
  • Chores
  • Downtime

What time-blocking is: Dividing the day into focused segments for different activities. Helps prevent burnout and ensures all priorities are addressed.

2 Assign Specific Activities to Each Block

What to do: Be specific about what happens during each time block

Don't forget: Include travel and transition times between blocks

Example: Instead of saying yes to every sewing project, assess your current workload and give realistic timelines or decline if overbooked

3 Set Strict Boundaries for Each Block

What to do: Avoid spillover from one block into another

"Set strict boundaries so you're giving all the time you need to all the things."

What boundaries are: Limits set to protect your time and energy. For you, this means saying no or setting realistic expectations for clients.

"You can say no, or say, 'I can't do it in two weeks.'"

4 Adjust Blocks as Needed

What to do: Modify for weekends or special projects

Tip: Keep work-related tasks mostly during the week to protect weekends for rest

Build in: Daily routines for cleaning, meals, and downtime

Why this framework matters:

Ensures all priorities get attention — Nothing gets neglected

Prevents overcommitment — You can see when you're at capacity

Builds in recovery time — Rest is scheduled, not an afterthought

How this applies to you: You already have routines for work, sewing, and gym, but sometimes overlaps or overcommits. Structuring your evenings and weekends with clear blocks will help maintain balance.

"Structure. You're doing the same stuff, but with more intention."

Recovery Is Essential

"Make sure you get your recovery and all that kind of stuff because it's a lot to be up and active all the time."

What recovery is: Allowing time for rest and rejuvenation to maintain long-term productivity

Schedule it intentionally: Recovery is as important as productivity

🌱 Framework #3: Personal Development Time

"Personal development time is about processing things for yourself, not just doing more work."

The Three-Step Process

1 Schedule Regular Sessions

What to do: Set aside at least one session per week for reflection, planning, and skill-building

Make it non-negotiable: Protect this time like any other important appointment

2 Use This Time to Process

What to focus on:

  • Business ideas
  • Work challenges
  • Personal goals
  • Strategic thinking
  • Learning and growth

What personal development is: Time set aside for self-reflection, learning, and planning, distinct from routine work or hobbies

"All the processes, all the things you think about — just setting up your life so it's progressing toward your goals."

"It's that personal development, obviously, developing your person."

3 Keep This Separate from "Second Job" Activities

Important distinction: This is separate from sewing or gym activities

This is for: Reflection and planning, not just "doing"

Action step: Write down insights and action steps during this time

Why this framework matters:

Personal development is about intentional growth, not just productivity

Keeps you aligned with your long-term vision

How this applies to you: You tend to focus on tangible tasks (sewing, cleaning) but need to carve out time for strategic thinking and growth.

🛠️ Practical Instructions

Delegation Steps

  1. List all your business and work tasks
  2. Mark those only you can do
  3. For each, ask: "If I'm not here, who could do this? How can I train them?"
  4. Draw a network tree showing who is responsible for what
  5. Review and update monthly

Time-Blocking Steps

  1. Map out your typical weekday and weekend
  2. Assign blocks for work, business, gym, chores, personal development, and downtime
  3. Stick to boundaries — don't let one block eat into another
  4. Adjust as needed for special events or deadlines

Personal Development Steps

  1. Schedule at least one session per week
  2. Use this time for reflection, planning, or learning — not just "doing"
  3. Write down insights and action steps

What to Avoid

  • Taking on new projects without checking your current load
  • Letting work or business tasks spill into personal or recovery time
  • Neglecting recovery or downtime
