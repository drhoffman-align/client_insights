🛡️ Setting Boundaries and Honoring Your Time

Navigating Guilt, Annoyance, and Assertiveness

📍 The Situation: When Others' Needs Override Your Plans

"You get to decide what you do. And so you get to be irritated. You get to feel them."

The Recurring Pattern

What keeps happening: Others, especially family members, expect you to adjust your plans to accommodate their requests — often at the last minute

This week's example: Asked to pick up a passport for someone, which conflicted with your gym routine

How it felt:

  • Rushed
  • Your time was undervalued
  • Despite agreeing to help, you felt annoyed

What You're Working Toward

Your goal: More consistency and structure in your life, particularly with your gym routine

What you're recovering from: A period of inconsistency

Why this matters to you: Gym time is a key part of your self-care and structure

The Pressure Tactics

What your sister does: Subtle guilt-tripping and pressure

How she frames requests: In a way that makes you feel obligated or guilty if you don't comply

The result: You feel pressured to say yes even when it disrupts your plans

The core problem: Struggle to maintain your boundaries and prioritize your own needs when others make demands on your time.

What you experience: Annoyance and loss of focus when your plans are disrupted, especially when others don't acknowledge or respect your schedule.

The internal conflict: Wanting to be helpful but resenting the lack of consideration for your own needs.

The pattern: Tendency to suppress or "let go" of these feelings until they accumulate and resurface with new incidents.

🎯 Framework #1: Using Emotions as Strategy

"Use your feelings as strategy to help you think about how to handle things."

The Five-Step Process

1 Notice and Name the Emotion

What you're feeling: Annoyance, irritation, frustration

In the passport example: You felt annoyed

Why naming matters: You can't address what you haven't identified

2 Ask Yourself Why You Feel This Way

What is the trigger?

In your case:

  • Your gym time was being dismissed as unimportant
  • Your schedule wasn't considered
  • The request was last-minute
  • You felt your time was undervalued

The deeper issue: Your boundary (gym time) was being crossed

3 Validate the Feeling

Ask: Is this reasonable given the situation?

The therapist's validation: Yes, your annoyance is valid. Your time and priorities matter.

Important truth: "Just because it's a priority for you doesn't mean it's a priority for the next person."

Why validation matters: Acknowledging that your feelings are real and reasonable prevents you from suppressing them

4 Decide How to Respond

Your options:

  • Express the feeling directly: Talk to the person
  • Write it out: Process it in your notes app
  • Set a boundary: "I can't help during my gym time"

The therapist's guidance: "You don't always have to express yourself, but you should always think about how."

5 Prepare for Future Situations

What to do: Plan your response for next time

Examples:

  • "I'm not available during my gym time, but I can help after"
  • "I need more notice to adjust my schedule"
  • "That doesn't work for me today"

Why this matters: Having prepared responses reduces in-the-moment pressure to say yes

✓ Why This Framework Matters

Emotions are signals that something needs attention

By identifying and validating them, you can use them to guide your actions

Prevents emotions from building up or leading to passive-aggressive behavior

💬 Framework #2: Direct Communication and Boundary Setting

"People will keep stepping over your boundaries if you don't handle it."

The Four-Step Process

1 Clearly State Your Feeling and the Reason

The formula: "I felt [emotion] because [reason]"

Example from your situation: "I was annoyed because my time wasn't considered"

Why this works:

  • Direct and clear
  • Focuses on your experience, not accusations
  • Helps others understand your perspective

2 Avoid Adding Assumptions About Others' Intentions

Don't assume: "They think I have nothing to do because I'm not working"

Unless: They explicitly state that

The therapist's guidance: "Try not to add what other people may be thinking unless it's explicitly said."

Why this matters: Adding assumptions inflates negative emotions and isn't grounded in reality

3 Ask Clarifying Questions to Open Dialogue

Instead of assuming, ask:

  • "Was there a reason you needed it earlier?"
  • "Is there flexibility in timing?"
  • "Can this wait until after my gym time?"

Why this works: Opens conversation instead of creating conflict

4 Practice Expressing Boundaries in Writing First

If direct conversation feels too difficult: Write it out in your notes app

The therapist's insight: "Writing it down gives you an opportunity to feel like you're saying what you need to say."

What to write:

  • What happened
  • How you felt
  • Why you felt that way
  • How you might express this to the person

Why this works: Builds confidence for real conversations, processes feelings, clarifies your thoughts

✓ Why Direct Communication Matters

Prevents resentment from building up

Helps others understand your perspective

Setting boundaries protects your time and energy

Practicing in writing builds confidence for speaking

🔍 Understanding Key Dynamics

Boundary Setting

What it means: Clearly defining what you are and are not willing to do

In your life, this looks like:

  • Sticking to your gym schedule
  • Not letting others' requests override your plans
  • Protecting your personal time

The reminder: "Setting boundaries protects your time and energy."

Guilt-Tripping

What it is: When someone tries to make you feel bad for not doing what they want

How it shows up: Implying you're selfish or uncaring if you don't comply

Your sister's pattern: Uses language that makes you feel guilty for prioritizing yourself

The truth: Guilt-tripping language from others is about their needs, not your obligations

Conflict Avoidance

What it is: Not addressing issues directly, often to keep the peace

Your pattern: Tend to let things go rather than express your annoyance

The problem: This leads to resentment

The therapist's guidance: "Conflict isn't always bad. A lot of us are conflict avoidant, but we gotta handle it."

The reframe: Conflict can be healthy and necessary for maintaining boundaries

Validation

What it is: Acknowledging that your feelings are real and reasonable

The therapist's repeated validation: Your annoyance is understandable given the circumstances

Why this matters: Prevents you from suppressing legitimate feelings

🛠️ Practical Tools and Instructions

When You Feel Annoyed or Pressured

  1. Pause and name the emotion
  2. Ask yourself why you feel this way — what boundary is being crossed?
  3. Decide if you want to express the feeling directly or write it out first

Use Direct, Simple Language

Format: "I felt annoyed because my time wasn't considered"

Not: Long explanations or justifications

Avoid Making Assumptions

Don't add: What others might be thinking unless they say it out loud

Keeps emotions grounded in reality

Use "Do Not Disturb" Mode

When: During important personal time (gym, self-care)

Why: Protects your focus and prevents interruptions

The result: You can complete your priorities without being pulled away

Practice Writing in Your Notes App

What to write: Your feelings, even if you don't send them

Purpose: Process emotions, clarify thoughts, rehearse communication

Ask Open-Ended Questions

If you choose to address the issue: Use questions to invite dialogue

Example: "Was there a reason you needed it earlier?"

Remember This Important Truth

You don't have to act on every emotion, but practicing expression builds skill

📝 Your Assignment

Practice Writing Out Your Emotional Responses

What to do: Use the notes section of your phone whenever you feel annoyed, pressured, or upset by someone's request

Steps:

  1. When you notice a strong emotion, open your notes app
  2. Write down what happened, how you felt, and why
  3. Draft how you might express this to the person involved, focusing on "I felt X because Y"
  4. Reflect on whether you want to share it or keep it for your own processing

Purpose: To process your feelings, clarify your boundaries, and rehearse direct communication without immediate confrontation

What to bring next time: Examples of situations where you used this strategy and how it felt. Be ready to discuss any patterns or challenges you noticed.

Timeline: Ongoing — use this strategy whenever relevant over the next two weeks until your next session
