🧠 Managing Medication, Anxiety, and Self-Perception

Building Awareness and Resilience

📍 The Situation: Adjusting to Medication and Managing Anxiety

"Brain does a thing that makes things issues that aren't issues if you make it a issue."

What You're Currently Navigating

  • Adjusting to ADHD medication
  • Experiencing some side effects (particularly changes in appetite and anxiety)
  • Managing daily life stressors and relationships
  • Ongoing process of finding the right medication dosage

Your Thinking Pattern

What happens: You tend to overthink bodily sensations and symptoms

The result: This leads to increased anxiety

The cycle: Hyper-focus on sensations → interpretation as potential problems → amplified anxiety

Underlying Self-Perception Issues

What you experience: History of self-critical thinking and uncertainty about self-worth

Possible roots: Past experiences in sports and academics

The core problem: Tendency to over-attend to bodily sensations and symptoms, interpreting minor or expected side effects as potential problems.

How this amplifies anxiety: Hyper-focus makes normal sensations feel problematic, leading to unnecessary worry.

The underlying pattern: Self-doubt and negative self-perception, possibly reinforced by past experiences and current stressors.

The mismatch: Your perception that every sensation could signal a problem vs. reality that many are normal or manageable.

🔍 Framework #1: Symptom Assessment Framework

"Don't worry about the medication unless it is redundant and painful or uncomfortable."

The Five-Step Process

1 Notice the Symptom or Sensation

What to do: Simply acknowledge what you're experiencing

Examples: Appetite changes, dry mouth, feeling different

2 Ask: Is It Redundant/Repetitive, Painful, or Uncomfortable?

Three key questions:

  • Redundant/repetitive: Is this happening over and over?
  • Painful: Does this actually hurt?
  • Uncomfortable: Is this genuinely disrupting your life?

3 Determine If It Is Abnormal or Irregular for You

Ask yourself: Is this completely out of the ordinary for my body?

Or: Is this a known side effect of my medication?

4 If Not Severe or Disruptive, Acknowledge It and Move On

The therapist's guidance: "If it is not a thing that is redundant, repetitive, or absolutely a problem, then paying attention to it is like most people notice things when they're not in a problem."

What this means: Notice it, acknowledge it, then let it go

Don't: Dwell on it, analyze it, worry about it

5 If Severe, Persistent, or Causes Significant Distress, Report It

Report symptoms that are:

  • Severe (really impacting your functioning)
  • Persistent (won't go away, keep coming back)
  • Painful or very uncomfortable
  • Causing significant distress

Example threshold: "If you have a panic attack or just can't get rid of your anxiety, that's probably around the threshold."

✓ Why This Framework Matters

Helps distinguish between normal medication side effects and symptoms requiring attention

Reduces unnecessary anxiety

Promotes self-efficacy — you can assess and manage on your own

💭 Framework #2: Managing Anxious Thinking

"Try not to make it more than what it is because that's what you're used to sort of thinking."

The Four-Step Process

1 Notice When You Are Focusing on a Sensation or Worry

Catch yourself in the act: "Oh, I'm dwelling on this sensation"

Awareness is the first step

2 Ask Yourself If You Are Making It a Bigger Issue Than It Is

The key question: "Am I making this into a problem when it's actually normal?"

Your pattern: "Brain does a thing that makes things issues that aren't issues if you make it a issue."

3 Practice Letting Go of Thoughts That Are Not Urgent or Problematic

What to tell yourself: "This is just a sensation. It's not an emergency. I can let this go."

Remember: Not every sensation or thought needs to be analyzed

4 Redirect Attention to the Present or Something Constructive

Shift focus to:

  • What you're doing right now
  • A task at hand
  • Something engaging or enjoyable
  • Your breath or surroundings

✓ Why This Process Matters

Interrupts the cycle of anxious rumination

Helps develop more balanced perspective on bodily sensations and daily experiences

Reduces overthinking that amplifies anxiety

🪞 Framework #3: Self-Reflection and Identity Building

"It's better to have a running list on your phone. Anytime you think about, 'Oh, I'm really nice,' then you can pull it out and just kind of write it down."

The Four-Step Process

1 Regularly Note Characteristics About Yourself

What to capture: Any trait, quality, or characteristic you notice

When: Whenever something comes to mind

2 Include Both Your Own Perceptions and What Others Say About You

Two sources of information:

  • Your observations: "I notice I'm patient with people"
  • Others' feedback: "My friend said I'm a good listener"

3 Be Specific, Noting Both Positive and Negative Traits

Include everything: Not just the good stuff

Examples:

  • "I'm creative when solving problems"
  • "I sometimes get defensive when criticized"
  • "People say I'm reliable"
  • "I tend to overthink things"

Why include negatives: Builds balanced, realistic self-view

4 Keep a Running List

Where to keep it: Your phone or a journal

How to use it: Add to it whenever something occurs to you, review periodically

The therapist's suggestion: "Get a journal so you can kinda talk to yourself."

✓ Why This Exercise Matters

Builds self-awareness

Challenges negative self-perceptions

Provides resource for future reflection and growth

Counteracts old patterns of self-doubt

💊 Understanding Your ADHD Medication

Current Status

Current dosage: 10mg

Possible range: Up to 30mg as needed

Timing: Take as prescribed, monitor effects throughout the day

Purpose and Expected Benefits

Purpose: Improve focus and reduce ADHD symptoms

Expected benefits: Improved focus, reduced anxiety during the day

Common Side Effects to Expect

  • Appetite changes: Common and usually not problematic
  • Dry mouth: Actually a good indicator the medication is active

What to monitor: Any severe or persistent symptoms

Understanding How Medication Should Feel

The therapist's explanation: "You should not feel like you first took it. You should start to get used to it, but you should still get... well, dry mouth is a really good indicator of that."

What this means:

  • You won't feel the same "first dose" sensation over time
  • You should get used to being on it
  • Dry mouth is a good sign it's working
  • If dry mouth disappears AND you don't feel effects, discuss with provider

About Tolerance and Dosage

Important clarification: Not feeling the medication's effects does NOT mean you're developing tolerance this early

More likely: The dose may need adjustment

The therapist's reminder: "Everybody's body is different. Ten milligrams is... that's why it goes all the way up to thirty milligrams per tablet."

Dosage is individualized: What works for someone else may not be your perfect dose

Safety Warnings

  • Do not increase dose without consulting the provider
  • Report any severe, persistent, or painful side effects
  • Addiction is not expected to be an issue at this stage

Communication with Your Provider

The approach: "Trial and error, and you tell me."

What to communicate:

  • If effects wear off during the day
  • If you're not noticing effects (and dry mouth is gone)
  • If side effects become severe or disruptive

Remember: Your feedback is essential for finding the right dose
