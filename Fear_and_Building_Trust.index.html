  • Fear that mistakes mean you're incompetent
  • Anxiety about what could go wrong despite your best efforts

The mismatch: Your actual competence (successful surgery, handling difficult situations) vs. your feelings of inadequacy and fear.

🎯 The Core Problem: Internalizing Blame Without Seeking Understanding

"Mistakes are normal human responses, not just personal failures."

Your Tendency: Absorbing Blame

What you do: Internalize mistakes and take full responsibility without seeking context or understanding

How this shows up:

  • Accept reprimands without questioning whether they're proportionate
  • Assume the problem is entirely your fault
  • Don't communicate your perspective or ask for clarification
  • Withdraw instead of engaging in dialogue
  • Turn external criticism inward as self-doubt and anxiety

The therapist's observation: You struggle to see your errors as normal human responses

The Perfectionism Trap

Your belief: Safety = Perfection

What this creates:

  • Chronic stress and fear of mistakes
  • Belief that only flawless performance is acceptable
  • Heightened anxiety when things don't go perfectly
  • Reluctance to communicate in high-pressure environments
  • Self-doubt despite demonstrated competence

The truth: "No matter how much preparation you do, it will never be perfect — and that's okay."

The Communication Gap

Before (old pattern): Accept reprimands without question, internalize fault, withdraw

What this costs:

  • Increases anxiety and self-doubt
  • Prevents understanding of actual protocols and expectations
  • Misses opportunities to clarify your intentions
  • Doesn't build trust or mutual understanding
  • Leaves you feeling unsafe and unsupported

After (new approach): Seek understanding, communicate your perspective, use mistakes as learning opportunities

⚠️ The Cost of Internalizing Blame

  • Heightened anxiety
  • Undermined self-confidence
  • Chronic stress from perfectionism
  • Feeling unsafe even when competent
  • Missing context that could reduce fear

🤝 Framework #1: Trust-Building (Maslow's Hierarchy Focus)

"Trust is built through time, consistency, and experiences."

The Five-Step Process

1 Recognize That Safety and Security Are Foundational Needs

Maslow's Hierarchy: Basic needs (like safety) must be met before higher-level growth (like self-esteem) can occur

In your life:

  • The police stop threatened your sense of physical safety
  • The hospital incident threatened your professional security
  • Without feeling safe, anxiety and self-doubt flourish
  • You can't fully thrive when your foundation feels shaky

The therapist's insight: "Safety and security are at the core of being — at the bottom of Maslow's hierarchy of needs"

2 Understand That Trust Is a Pillar of Safety and Security

What this means: You can't feel safe without trust — both in yourself and in your environment

Why trust matters:

  • Trust creates psychological safety
  • Without it, every situation feels potentially threatening
  • Lack of trust amplifies anxiety about mistakes
  • Trust allows you to take risks necessary for growth

3 Trust Is Built Through Time, Consistency, and Shared Experiences

What this means: Trust doesn't happen instantly — it develops gradually

How trust builds:

  • Time: Repeated interactions over weeks and months
  • Consistency: Reliable behavior from both parties
  • Shared experiences: Working through challenges together
  • Communication: Openness and transparency
  • Demonstrated competence: Showing you can handle situations

In your situation: You're still relatively new at this hospital — trust with administration and colleagues is still developing

4 Prioritize Self-Trust, But Also Work to Build Mutual Trust with Others

Self-trust first: "It's easier or better to trust yourself over others"

Building self-trust:

  • Validate your competence: "You are capable, and your abilities to handle things are pretty good"
  • Remember positive outcomes: The surgery was successful
  • Trust your judgment and intentions
  • Recognize you handled difficult situations

Building trust with others:

  • Requires more time, consistency, and experiences
  • Be patient with the process
  • Show up consistently and communicate openly
  • Let others see your competence and good intentions

5 Use Transparency and Open Communication to Foster Trust

What this means: Share your perspective, intentions, and questions — not as defense, but as connection

The therapist's guidance: "Use transparency not as a protective mechanism, but as a way to build understanding"

How to apply this:

  • Follow up with administration to discuss the incident
  • Express your intention to learn and understand protocols
  • Share your perspective on what happened
  • Ask questions to clarify expectations
  • Show you're committed to growth and improvement

✓ Why This Framework Matters

Creates foundation: Safety and trust are prerequisites for self-esteem and thriving

Reduces anxiety: When you trust yourself and your environment, fear decreases

Enables growth: You can learn from mistakes instead of being paralyzed by them

Builds relationships: Mutual trust creates more secure professional environment

🌟 Framework #2: Experiential Understanding

"You can read about it in a book, but until you actually show up and experience the thing, you don't completely understand."

The Three-Step Process

1 Acknowledge That Knowledge Alone Is Not Enough

What this means: You can prepare extensively, but experience teaches what preparation cannot

Your realization: "No matter how much preparation, it will never be perfect"

Why this matters: This isn't failure — it's reality. No amount of reading or planning captures the full complexity of real situations

2 Combine Intellectual Knowledge with Emotional Experience

What this means: True understanding comes from both thinking AND feeling

The therapist's insight: "Experiences bring about understanding — through both knowledge and feelings"

In your situation:

  • You knew protocols intellectually
  • But experiencing the miscommunication taught you more
  • The emotional response (anxiety, fear) is part of the learning
  • Both cognitive and emotional data create fuller understanding

3 Seek Out Opportunities to Show Up and Engage Directly

What this means: Don't withdraw or avoid — engage to deepen understanding

Instead of avoiding:

  • Reach out to administration
  • Ask questions about protocols and expectations
  • Learn directly from those who set the standards
  • Show up to meetings and discussions
  • Engage with your team about what happened

Why this matters: Direct experience allows for fuller, more nuanced understanding of situations and systems. It also helps others understand YOU, which is essential for building trust and reducing misunderstandings.

💬 Framework #3: Communication and Reframing Mistakes

"Help me understand it. I am new here, and I would like to learn more."

The Three-Step Process

1 When a Mistake Occurs, Pause to Understand the Context

Questions to ask yourself:

  • What actually happened?
  • What was my intention?
  • What factors contributed to this situation?
  • Was this entirely my fault, or were there system issues?
  • What did I do well, even if the outcome wasn't perfect?

2 Communicate Openly, Focusing on Learning Rather Than Blame

How to communicate:

  • "Help me understand how this is usually handled. I am new here, and I would like to learn more."
  • "Can we discuss what happened so I can understand the protocol better?"
  • "I want to learn from this. What should I do differently next time?"
  • "Here's my perspective on what occurred. I'm curious about yours."

Tone to use: Respectful, curious, open to learning — not defensive or apologetic without cause

3 Use Mistakes as Opportunities for Growth and Relationship-Building

Reframe: Mistakes aren't just failures — they're chances to connect and improve

What mistakes can build:

  • Understanding: Of protocols, expectations, and systems
  • Trust: By showing you care about doing better
  • Relationships: Through open, honest communication
  • Resilience: By learning you can handle imperfection
  • Competence: Each mistake teaches something valuable

Why this matters: Open communication helps prevent escalation, clarifies intentions, and builds mutual respect. Reframing mistakes as learning opportunities reduces shame and increases resilience.
