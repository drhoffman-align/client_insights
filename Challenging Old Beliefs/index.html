✨ Rewriting the Narrative

Challenging Old Beliefs and Building Self-Compassion

📍 The Situation: The Gap Between Feeling and Reality

"You don't actually consider all the points in reality when you think about how terrible your life is and how pathetic you might be."

What You're Experiencing

Your current struggles:

  • Persistent feelings of loneliness and self-criticism
  • Belief that you're "pathetic" or unworthy of connection
  • History of having few friends weighing heavily on you
  • Anxiety — sometimes severe enough to require medication
  • Managing multiple medications for mental health and ADHD
  • Recently moved away from suicidal ideation (significant progress)

Where These Feelings Come From

Childhood experiences shaping your self-view:

  • Social exclusion at school — being made fun of
  • Parental criticism about not having friends
  • Being criticized FOR not having friends created a belief that this makes you "pathetic"
  • These experiences created deeply ingrained beliefs about your worth

The Reality Others See

What's actually true about you NOW:

  • Well-liked at work by students and colleagues
  • Actively fosters kindness and inclusion among students
  • Students seek you out to talk and connect
  • Creates a supportive environment for others
  • Capable of forming meaningful connections when you allow yourself to

The therapist's observation: "Most of the kids don't actually get people to talk to them and try and understand them." — But they come to YOU.

The gap: How you FEEL about yourself ("I am pathetic and friendless") vs. REALITY (you are valued, liked, and capable of connection).

The mismatch: Your negative self-narrative doesn't match the evidence from your current life.

The pattern: Old beliefs from childhood are preventing you from seeing your present reality clearly.

🎯 The Core Problem: Living in an Outdated Story

"The idea that you cannot make friends is only perpetuated by the fact that you don't make friends because you don't go out to make friends."

The Internalized Belief

What you believe about yourself: "I am fundamentally unworthy of friendship or connection"

Where this belief comes from:

  • Childhood social exclusion
  • Being made fun of at school
  • Parents criticizing you for not having friends
  • Learning to see yourself through others' judgments

How this belief is maintained:

  • Avoiding social opportunities (which prevents new evidence)
  • Dismissing work relationships as "not real friends"
  • Focusing only on what's lacking, not what exists
  • Automatic acceptance of negative self-talk

Evidence AGAINST This Belief (That You're Ignoring)

What's actually happening in your life:

  • You ARE forming connections at work
  • Students actively seek you out
  • Colleagues value you
  • You create inclusion and kindness for others
  • You have the skills to connect — you demonstrate them daily

The therapist's challenge: "Have you thought about how pathetic you might NOT be since you can make friends everywhere else?"

The "Work Friends" Limitation

What you tell yourself: "These are just work friends, not REAL friends"

The therapist's reframe: "You just see them as work friends which is something some other, like, some meaning you give."

The truth: Work friends ARE real friends. The distinction is artificial — a way of protecting yourself from acknowledging your capability to connect

Why this matters: You're capable of connection but limit yourself by not extending relationships outside work or giving them full credit

The Avoidance Cycle

How the pattern reinforces itself:

1. You believe you're unworthy of friendship

2. This creates anxiety about social situations

3. You avoid social opportunities (meet-ups, groups)

4. No new experiences = no new evidence

5. Old belief stays unchallenged

6. You continue feeling lonely and "pathetic"

7. Belief is "confirmed"

8. Repeat

The key insight: Behavioral avoidance keeps old beliefs in place because there's no new evidence to challenge them

⚠️ The Cost of This Pattern

  • Persistent loneliness despite being capable of connection
  • Dismissing real connections you DO have
  • Missing opportunities for friendship
  • Reinforcing negative self-beliefs
  • Living in a story from the past, not your present reality

🔍 Framework #1: Reality Testing Negative Beliefs

"All I'm doing is saying... we do have friends."

The Four-Step Process

1 Identify the Negative Belief

What this means: Name the specific thought you're having about yourself

Your belief: "I am pathetic because I don't have friends"

Other versions of this belief:

  • "I'm unworthy of connection"
  • "I can't make friends"
  • "I'm fundamentally flawed"
  • "People don't like me"

Why this step matters: You can't challenge a belief until you clearly identify it

2 Gather Evidence For and Against the Belief

What this means: Look at ACTUAL facts from your current life, not feelings or old stories

Evidence FOR "I can't make friends":

  • I don't have many friends outside of work
  • I feel lonely sometimes
  • I had few friends growing up

Evidence AGAINST "I can't make friends":

  • Students actively seek me out to talk and connect
  • Colleagues value me and enjoy working with me
  • I foster kindness and inclusion — people respond positively
  • "Most kids don't get people to talk to them" — but they come to ME
  • I'm well-liked at work across multiple relationships
  • I CHOOSE not to extend work relationships (not that I can't)
  • I avoid social opportunities like meet-ups (haven't actually tested if I can make friends there)

What this reveals: The evidence against the belief is much stronger than evidence for it

3 Actively Challenge the Belief by Considering Alternative Explanations

What this means: Look for other ways to interpret the situation

Old interpretation: "I don't have many friends outside work because I'm pathetic and unworthy"

Alternative explanations:

  • "I don't have many friends outside work because I haven't gone to places where I could meet people"
  • "I dismiss work relationships as 'not real' which limits me from deepening connections I already have"
  • "I avoid social situations because of anxiety and old fears, not because I'm incapable"
  • "The belief that I can't make friends is based on childhood experiences, not current reality"
  • "I AM making connections — I'm just not giving myself credit for them"

The therapist's reframe: "The idea that you cannot make friends is only perpetuated by the fact that you don't make friends because you don't go out to make friends."

4 Replace the Old Narrative with a More Balanced, Compassionate View

What this means: Create a new story based on evidence, not old beliefs

Old narrative:

"I am pathetic because I don't have friends. I'm fundamentally unworthy of connection. People don't really like me."

New, balanced narrative:

"I AM capable of forming meaningful connections, as shown by my relationships at work. Students seek me out, and colleagues value me. I struggle with expanding my social circle because of anxiety and old beliefs from childhood, NOT because I'm unworthy. When I put myself in social situations, I connect well. The work I need to do is challenge my avoidance and give myself credit for the connections I already have."

Why this matters: This process helps you see that self-criticism is based on outdated or incomplete information, not present-day reality

✓ What Reality Testing Gives You

Interrupts automatic negative self-talk: You stop believing every critical thought

Opens door to self-compassion: You can see yourself more kindly

Creates space for change: If the belief isn't true, you can act differently

Grounds you in current reality: Not childhood stories

🚀 Framework #2: Behavioral Activation for Social Connection

"Schedule one. And figure out how to get there. You should schedule one. You should look up one, any one. And figure out how to get there. And take a Xanax."

The Four-Step Process

1 Identify Opportunities for Social Engagement

What this means: Find specific, concrete places to meet people

Examples:

  • Meet-up groups (interests, hobbies, activities)
  • Community classes or workshops
  • Volunteer opportunities
  • Book clubs or discussion groups
  • Fitness classes or sports leagues
  • Religious or spiritual communities

Your task: Look up ONE meet-up or group event that interests you

2 Make a Concrete Plan to Attend

What this means: Schedule it, plan logistics — make it REAL

Your concrete plan should include:

  • Which event: Name and description
  • When: Date and time
  • Where: Exact location
  • How you'll get there: Drive, bus, ride-share
  • Put it on your calendar
  • Set a reminder

Why specificity matters: Vague plans don't happen. Concrete plans do.

3 Use Coping Tools as Needed

What this means: It's okay to use medication for anxiety — that's what it's for

Your available tools:

  • Xanax (as prescribed): For breakthrough anxiety before/during the event
  • Self-reassurance: Remind yourself you connect well at work
  • Reality testing: "This anxiety is old fear, not current danger"
  • Permission to leave: You can attend briefly if needed

The therapist's guidance: "Take a Xanax" — use your prescribed medication to support this step

Important: Medication is a tool for managing anxiety, not a solution for avoidance. Use it to DO the thing, not to avoid it.

4 Reflect on the Experience and Challenge Avoidance Patterns

What this means: After the event, compare what actually happened to what you feared

Questions to ask yourself:

  • What did I fear would happen?
  • What actually happened?
  • Did anyone reject me or treat me as "pathetic"?
  • Did I connect with anyone, even briefly?
  • What did this experience teach me about my belief?
  • What evidence did I gather?

Write it down: Document the experience and your reflections to bring to next session

✓ Why Behavioral Activation Works

Creates new experiences: That can disconfirm old beliefs

Gathers real evidence: Instead of operating on assumptions

Builds confidence: Even small social risks increase self-efficacy

Breaks avoidance cycle: Action interrupts the pattern that keeps beliefs stuck

Tests reality: You discover you're more capable than you believed

🛠️ Practical Instructions: What to Do

When You Notice Negative Self-Talk

Pause and ask: "What's the evidence for and against this?"

Example:

  • Thought: "I'm pathetic"
  • Pause: Stop and challenge it
  • Ask: "Is this actually true? What's the evidence?"
  • List: Examples that contradict it (students seek me out, colleagues value me, I foster inclusion)

Build Your Evidence List

What to do: Keep an ongoing list of examples from current life that contradict negative beliefs

Include:

  • Positive feedback from colleagues
  • Times students sought you out
  • Moments of connection at work
  • Evidence of your kindness and inclusion
  • Anything that shows you CAN and DO connect

Schedule a Social Activity

Specific steps:

  1. Search for a local meet-up or group event that interests you
  2. Choose ONE event
  3. Put it on your calendar
  4. Make a plan for how you'll get there
  5. Decide if you'll take Xanax before (as prescribed)
  6. Set a reminder
  7. Attend, even if only briefly

After the Event

Reflect and document:

  • What actually happened vs. what you feared
  • Any connections made, even small ones
  • How you felt during and after
  • What this taught you about your beliefs

⚠️ What to Avoid

  • Letting old narratives go unchallenged — Actively look for ways they might not be true
  • Dismissing work relationships — These ARE real friendships
  • Avoiding social opportunities — This keeps old beliefs in place
  • Only focusing on feelings, not facts — Look at actual evidence

Xanax (Alprazolam)

Dosage/Timing: Take only as needed for breakthrough panic attacks

Purpose: Rapid relief of acute anxiety/panic

How to take: Only when experiencing severe anxiety (like before social events)

Expected benefits: Quick reduction of panic symptoms

Side effects to monitor: Drowsiness, risk of dependence, possible cognitive slowing

Safety warnings: Use sparingly; do not drive after taking

Rationale: Reserved for emergencies and high-anxiety situations, not daily use

Vyvanse

Purpose: ADHD management

How to take: Daily, as prescribed

Expected benefits: Improved focus, attention, and executive function

Side effects to monitor: Insomnia, appetite suppression, possible anxiety

Safety warnings: Monitor for increased anxiety or heart symptoms

Rationale: Supports functioning at work and in daily life

Wellbutrin

Purpose: Depression management

How to take: Daily, as prescribed

Expected benefits: Improved mood, energy, and motivation

Side effects to monitor: Insomnia, dry mouth, possible anxiety

Safety warnings: Watch for increased agitation or mood swings

Rationale: Maintains mood stability
