👑 Reclaiming Power

Challenging Old Narratives and Building Self-Belief

📍 The Situation: When Old Stories Hold You Back

"If you don't believe in yourself, nobody will."

Your Current Financial and Marital Stress

What you're facing:

  • Husband unemployed for over a year
  • Family struggling to maintain high mortgage
  • Ongoing anxiety about stability

What you've done: Taken on gig work (driving) to contribute financially

The positive result: Improved your mood and sense of agency

Your concern: Feel this is only a temporary solution

Your Deep-Seated Insecurities

What you're wrestling with:

  • Career trajectory doubts
  • Lack of college degree
  • History of feeling undervalued or unsupported — both at home and in workplace

Compounding factors:

  • Father's longstanding criticism
  • Internalized belief that you are "mediocre" without formal credentials
The core problem: Internalization of negative narratives about your worth and capability, especially regarding career and financial independence.

What you tend to do: Avoid opportunities that might lead to greater responsibility or visibility.

What you fear: Both failure and the burden of being primary earner in a household with a financially irresponsible partner.

How this is reinforced: Past experiences of disappointment and criticism, leading to cycle where you underestimate your own achievements and hesitate to advocate for yourself.

"The way that you stay mediocre is not addressing the issues and feeling like you have power."

🔍 Understanding Key Concepts

Self-Narrative

What it is: The story you tell yourself about who you are and what you're capable of

In your case: Your self-narrative has been shaped by your father's criticism and societal expectations about degrees

Why it matters: This story shapes your self-esteem and choices

Avoidance

What it is: Choosing not to engage with tasks or situations that provoke anxiety

For you: Avoidance shows up in not applying for jobs or expanding your business

"You avoid thinking about it. You avoid processing it. And that is the one thing that you actually need to do to be successful."

Boundaries

What they are: Limits set to protect your well-being and resources

Your key example: Financial boundaries with your husband

What you've already done: Set boundaries (controlling spending, refusing to enable financial irresponsibility), which has improved your sense of agency

Reframing

What it is: Looking at a situation from a different, more empowering perspective

The therapist's reframe for you: Your patchy resume is evidence of adaptability and survival, not failure

Your varied work history: A strength, not a weakness

📖 Framework #1: The Power of Self-Narrative and Reframing

"You have to tell yourself the whole story."

The Four-Step Process

1 Identify the Dominant Story You Tell Yourself

Your current story: "I'm mediocre because I don't have a degree"

Why this matters: This story shapes your self-esteem and choices

2 Gather Evidence from Your Actual Experiences

What to look at: Jobs held, challenges overcome

Why this matters: Real-life evidence often contradicts the negative narrative

The truth: By listing all your work experiences, you can see the breadth of your skills, countering the belief that you're "done nothing"

3 Reframe the Story to Include Context and Your Strengths

New story: "I have survived and adapted through many challenges"

"You are somebody, obviously, a survivor, but someone who tries to just kinda get things done."

The therapist's reframe: Your resume reflects resourcefulness, not failure

Why this matters: A more complete story empowers you to see your value

4 Practice Telling This New Story to Yourself and Others

Where to use it: In cover letters, interviews

The metaphor: "Selling yourself to yourself" — importance of internal validation before seeking external approval

"Your job is to sell yourself — and actually not just to sell yourself to others, but to sell yourself to yourself."

Why this matters: Repetition builds belief and confidence

Key insights:

Your self-narrative shapes your confidence and choices — challenge the old story

Past criticism (from your father or others) does not define your worth

You have survived and adapted through many challenges — this is strength

Internal validation is the foundation for external success

"There is a reason that validates your experience and why it didn't work out."

⚡ Framework #2: Agency Through Boundaries and Action

"You have power regardless."

The Three-Step Process

1 Recognize Areas Where You Have Control

Examples for you:

  • Setting financial boundaries
  • Choosing work

Why this matters: Focusing on your sphere of influence reduces helplessness

"You have more agency than you realize — use it."

2 Set and Maintain Boundaries, Even When Uncomfortable

Example for you: Not allowing your husband to use your credit card

Why this matters: Boundaries protect your resources and self-respect

What you've learned: You are not responsible for your husband's financial habits

The result: Boundaries with your husband are necessary and empowering

3 Take Small, Concrete Actions That Reinforce Your Agency

Example: Listing your work experiences

Why this matters: Action disrupts avoidance and builds momentum

"Action disrupts avoidance and builds momentum."

Remember:

Avoidance keeps you stuck; small actions break the cycle

Facing your fears about work is necessary for growth

Success can be scary, but avoidance only reinforces old, limiting beliefs

🎯 Framework #3: Exposure to Avoided Tasks

"You avoid thinking about it. You avoid processing it. And that is the one thing that you actually need to do to be successful."

The Four-Step Process

1 Identify What You Are Avoiding

For you:

  • Updating your resume
  • Applying for jobs
  • Expanding your business

What drives this: Your avoidance of job applications and business development is directly tied to your fear of failure and internalized criticism

2 Break the Task into Manageable Steps

Example: Write down all jobs, even small ones

The approach: Don't judge or filter — write everything down

3 Complete the First Step, Regardless of Discomfort

Take action: Make the list, even if it feels uncomfortable

Remember: Small actions break the cycle of avoidance

4 Reflect on the Experience and Adjust as Needed

What to notice: How you feel after taking action

Why this matters: Facing avoided tasks reduces their power and increases self-efficacy

🛠️ Practical Instructions

List All Work Experiences

Sit down and write out every job or gig you've done, no matter how small or "unimportant" it seems.

Reframe Your Resume

Use your list to create a narrative that highlights your adaptability, resourcefulness, and breadth of experience.

Set Clear Financial Boundaries

Continue to control your own spending and refuse to enable your husband's financial habits.

Challenge Negative Self-Talk

When you catch yourself thinking "I'm mediocre" or "I haven't done anything," refer to your list of experiences.

Take One Step Toward Business/Job Search

Choose one small action (e.g., update LinkedIn, draft a cover letter) and complete it this week.

Reflect on Avoidance

Notice when you're avoiding something out of fear, and gently push yourself to take a small step forward.

Important reminders:

"It doesn't have to be valuable to anybody else but you."

Your experiences are valuable, even if they don't fit a traditional mold

Taking inventory of your achievements is the first step toward self-advocacy
