Therapeutic Insights

Alignment Psychiatry

The Situation

Ericka is experiencing emotional and physical exhaustion from chronically giving more than she receives in her relationships, particularly with her husband Tony and family members. After 20+ years of marriage, she feels unsupported despite clearly communicating her needs and Tony's recent efforts to change. She's also navigating grief from her mother's passing while struggling with guilt when friends offer reciprocal support.

"Chronic one-sided giving causes burnout; balanced relationships require reciprocity."

Core Problem

Chronic imbalance in energy exchange leaves Ericka depleted, especially in her marriage where Tony's attempts to meet her needs consistently miss the mark. She over-functions as the giver, struggles to receive help without orchestrating it, and questions whether the relationship incompatibility can be resolved.

Key Insight: Incompatibility in giving and receiving love creates persistent distress that affects both mental and physical wellbeing.

Clinical Concepts

Energy Exchange
Emotional, practical, or financial support flowing between people
Depletion
Exhaustion from giving more than receiving
Reciprocity
Mutual exchange of care in healthy relationships
Incompatibility
Misalignment in how partners give/receive support
Boundaries
Limits protecting your energy and preventing resentment

Practical Instructions

  • Communicate needs clearly without apologizing
  • Accept help without guilt, orchestrating, or immediate reciprocation
  • Map your relationships to see which ones drain you and which replenish you
  • Remember that you're not responsible for managing others' feelings
  • Recognize that past behavior predicts future behavior; change is possible but slow
  • Prioritize your wellbeing, even if it means redefining relationships
Remember: You have agency to choose what supports your health and happiness. Supportive friendships model healthy reciprocity.

Homework

Reflect on and write responses to:

  • Your overall goals for your relationship, life, and son
  • Your capacity to allow Tony 1-2 years to change
  • What's best for your mental/physical health regardless of Tony's changes

Key Takeaways

  • Chronic one-sided giving causes burnout; balanced relationships require reciprocity

  • Past behavior predicts future behavior; change is possible but slow

  • Accept help without guilt, orchestrating, or immediate reciprocation
  • Incompatibility in giving/receiving love creates persistent distress
  • You're not responsible for managing others' feelings
  • Prioritizing your wellbeing is valid, even if it means redefining relationships
  • Supportive friendships model healthy reciprocity
  • You have agency to choose what supports your health and happiness
Bottom Line: Your exhaustion is a signal that the energy exchange in your relationships is imbalanced. Healthy relationships require reciprocity, and you deserve to receive the same care and support you give. Prioritizing your wellbeing isn't selfish—it's essential. You have the agency to choose relationships and patterns that support your health and happiness.
