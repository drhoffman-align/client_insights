Therapeutic Insights

Alignment Psychiatry

The Situation

You struggle with chronic self-doubt and a lifelong pattern of people-pleasing rooted in past family experiences, particularly with your mother. You habitually make yourself "small" in social situations, prioritize others' comfort over your own needs, and view yourself through others' eyes, leading to feelings of being devalued. Despite being intelligent and accomplished, you do not feel worthy of power or recognition and have difficulty trusting others with your authentic self.

Core Problem

"You automatically use 'fawning' as a survival mechanism—constantly adapting to others, seeking approval, and avoiding conflict by making yourself less visible. This creates a mismatch between your actual capabilities and your internalized belief that you are unworthy of power or space."

Clinical Concepts

Fawning
Trauma response involving pleasing others to avoid harm or rejection
Power
Both capability and belief (by self and others) in that capability
Self-Validation
Affirming one's own worth without relying on others' approval
Survival Mechanisms
Automatic behaviors developed to cope with past threats

Practical Instructions

Notice fawning moments: When you catch yourself making yourself small or prioritizing others' comfort over your own needs, pause. Ask yourself: "What do I actually want or need in this moment?"

Practice self-validation: Instead of waiting for external approval, affirm your own worth. Remind yourself that you are the ultimate authority on your own experience.

Assert your perspective: Start small by expressing your opinions and needs in low-stakes situations. Gradually reclaim your space in relationships and conversations.

Homework

  • Write daily (10 minutes) about strengths, achievements, and positive qualities
  • List three accomplishments or moments of capability
  • Reflect on what you want and deserve
  • Bring observations about self-talk changes to next session

Key Takeaways

  • Fawning is a learned survival response from unsafe environments, not a character flaw
  • Making yourself small preemptively avoids rejection but limits authentic living
  • Power requires both capability and self-belief—if you don't believe in yourself, others won't either
  • Self-validation is essential; don't wait for external approval
  • Notice fawning moments and pause to consider your true needs
  • Writing about strengths rewires self-concept through repetition
  • Assert your perspective in small ways to reclaim space
  • Trust your own experience—nobody knows you better than yourself
  • Being helpful is positive only when it doesn't erase your needs
  • Changing beliefs requires intentionally telling yourself a different story
Bottom Line: You have spent a lifetime making yourself small to survive, but survival mode is no longer serving you. Your capabilities are real, your needs matter, and you deserve to take up space. The path forward involves recognizing fawning patterns, challenging the false belief that you are unworthy of power, and intentionally rewriting your self-narrative through daily practice. Trust yourself—you are the expert on your own experience, and reclaiming your authentic self begins with believing in your own worth.
