Therapeutic Insights

Alignment Psychiatry

The Situation

You're experiencing heightened anxiety, excessive worry, and feelings of guilt and inadequacy, particularly in your relationships with family and a romantic interest. You find yourself apologizing frequently, ruminating over past events, and struggling with recurring thoughts that feel difficult to control.

Additionally, you're managing significant physical symptoms—explosive diarrhea, nausea, and fatigue—with a possible Crohn's disease diagnosis pending. You've noticed that stress directly impacts your physical health, creating a challenging cycle between your emotional and physical wellbeing.

"Feelings are valid but not always factual—seek evidence."

Core Problem

You're interpreting ambiguous social situations—especially perceived rejection—as evidence of personal inadequacy. This means you're conflating others' choices with your own worth. This cognitive pattern creates cycles of self-doubt, emotional distress, and avoidance behaviors.

There's a significant mismatch between what you're feeling ("he was turned off by me") and the actual evidence available to support that conclusion. This gap between feelings and facts is maintaining your distress.

Clinical Concepts

  • Rumination
    Repetitive, unproductive thinking about distressing topics that keeps you stuck in cycles of worry without resolution.
  • Black-and-White Thinking
    Seeing situations in extremes rather than nuances—things are either perfect or terrible, with no middle ground.
  • Rejection Sensitivity
    A heightened emotional response to perceived rejection, often leading to preemptive withdrawal or intense distress.
  • Evidence-Based Thinking
    Making judgments based on observable facts rather than assumptions, feelings, or interpretations.

Clinical Rationale: Separating feelings from facts interrupts automatic negative thoughts and reduces emotional intensity in ambiguous situations. This practice builds emotional resilience and supports healthier relationship patterns over time.

Practical Instructions

When you notice yourself ruminating or feeling overwhelmed:

  • Pause and grab your journal
  • List every emotion you're experiencing without judgment
  • Look for overlapping emotions and condense them (e.g., "anxious," "worried," and "nervous" might all be "fear")
  • For each core emotion, write: "I feel [emotion] because..."
  • Ask: "What observable evidence do I have for this belief?"
  • Write alternative explanations that fit the same facts
  • Practice holding both truths: your feeling AND the uncertainty

Remember: The goal isn't to eliminate feelings, but to examine whether they're based on facts or assumptions. This creates space between stimulus and response.

"Someone choosing another person doesn't indicate your inadequacy. Your worth isn't determined by others' choices."

Homework

Journal using the condensed emotion method: list emotions, condense overlapping ones, write why for each, ask for evidence, and reframe with alternatives
Bring one journal entry where you challenged a negative belief with evidence to your next session
Complete at least one entry before your next appointment
Important Note: If you begin biologic medication for Crohn's disease (pending blood work), follow your GI specialist's instructions carefully and report any severe reactions immediately. Managing stress through these therapeutic techniques is crucial for both your mental health and Crohn's disease management.

Key Takeaways

  • Feelings are valid but not always factual—seek evidence
  • Condensing emotions reduces overwhelm and clarifies true feelings
  • Someone choosing another person doesn't indicate your inadequacy
  • Hold both truths: your feeling and the uncertainty
  • Stress management is crucial for both mental health and Crohn's disease
  • Use observable facts, not assumptions, to judge situations
  • Rejection sensitivity can trigger preemptive withdrawal
  • Rumination keeps you stuck; structured journaling breaks the cycle
  • Your worth isn't determined by others' choices
Bottom Line: You're learning to distinguish between what you feel and what you know. By examining the evidence behind your beliefs, you can interrupt cycles of rumination and self-doubt. This isn't about dismissing your feelings—it's about ensuring they're grounded in reality rather than assumptions. As you practice these skills, you'll build resilience, reduce emotional reactivity, and develop healthier patterns in your relationships. Remember: uncertainty is uncomfortable, but it's also honest. You're capable of holding both your feelings and the truth that you may not have all the answers.
