Therapeutic Insights

Alignment Psychiatry

The Situation

You are navigating significant emotional processing triggered by personal experiences and global events, including grief, anxiety, and anger following a recent staycation and witnessing a military air show. You're working to establish consistent routines—sleep-wake schedule, strength training, dance—and integrate regular personal development time. The challenge lies in processing emotions consistently rather than only during breaks, and grappling with how to participate in collective action while managing feelings of uncertainty and overwhelm.

Core Problem

You experience feelings of insignificance and overwhelm when confronted with global events and displays of power. There's a tendency to absorb others' chaos and emotions, leading to difficulty setting boundaries and creating tension between your desire to help and your need for self-protection.

"Empathy doesn't require absorbing others' chaos. Boundaries are essential for sustainable compassion."

Clinical Concepts

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT): A therapeutic approach that examines how core beliefs shape thoughts, feelings, and actions. By understanding these connections, we can identify patterns and create meaningful change.

Core Beliefs: Deep-seated ideas about yourself and the world that drive automatic responses. These beliefs often operate beneath conscious awareness but powerfully influence how you interpret events and respond emotionally.

Emotional Processing: The practice of allowing yourself to feel and express emotions through journaling, movement, or creative expression. This prevents emotional buildup and promotes integration.

Boundaries: The practice of separating your own emotions and responsibilities from others'. Healthy boundaries allow you to care without becoming overwhelmed by others' experiences.

Decision Fatigue: The exhaustion that comes from making too many changes simultaneously. Sustainable change happens when we focus on one or two key areas at a time.

Therapeutic Frameworks

CBT Think-Feel-Do Model

1
Event Occurs: Something happens in your environment or life—a trigger, situation, or experience.
2
Thoughts Arise: Your mind generates judgments, assumptions, and interpretations about the event, filtered through your core beliefs.
3
Feelings Are Generated: Your thoughts create emotional responses—anxiety, anger, sadness, overwhelm, or other feelings.
4
Actions Follow: Your feelings drive behaviors and choices, which then create new events and continue the cycle.
"By identifying the thoughts and beliefs at step 2, you gain the power to shift your emotional experience and choose different actions."

Emotional Energy as Information

Emotions generate energy that serves as valuable information about your needs, values, and boundaries. This energy can be harnessed for purposeful action—like advocacy, creative expression, or meaningful conversation—or it can signal the need for rest, reflection, and self-care. Learning to read and respond to this information is a key skill in emotional wellness.

Practical Instructions

  • Prioritize your sleep-wake routine: Before adding new changes, solidify this foundation. Consistent sleep timing supports emotional regulation and decision-making.
  • Use journaling to slow your thoughts: During your personal development times, write freely about what you're feeling. This practice clarifies emotions and reveals underlying thoughts and beliefs.
  • Apply the Think-Feel-Do Model: When you notice strong emotions, pause and work backward. What are you feeling? What thoughts preceded those feelings? What beliefs might be driving those thoughts?
  • Practice boundary-setting: Notice when you're absorbing others' emotions. Remind yourself that caring about someone doesn't require taking on their chaos. You can be compassionate while maintaining separation.
  • Harness emotional energy purposefully: When emotions arise, ask: "What is this feeling telling me? Does it call for action or for rest?" Then respond intentionally rather than reactively.
  • Track patterns: Note your emotional state alongside your routine adherence. This helps identify what supports your wellbeing and what depletes it.

Homework

  • Continue journaling during personal development times (10am-12pm Mon-Thurs, 8-9:30pm Mon/Wed/Thurs)
  • Identify and explore feelings of insignificance, overwhelm, and boundary-setting in your journal
  • Send your current time block schedule for review
  • Reflect on recent emotional experiences and the underlying thoughts and beliefs that may have triggered them
  • Track your emotional state and how it relates to your routine changes

Key Takeaways

  • Feelings are important signals providing valuable information about your needs and values
  • Journaling slows thoughts and clarifies emotions, preventing buildup and overwhelm
  • Core beliefs shape your reactions; identifying them enables meaningful change
  • Emotional energy can fuel purposeful action or signal the need for rest—learn to read which is needed
  • Boundaries are essential—empathy doesn't require absorbing others' chaos
  • Focus on solidifying your sleep-wake routine before adding additional changes
  • You play a meaningful role in your community despite feelings of insignificance
  • Use the CBT framework to understand and shift your responses to challenging situations
  • Self-awareness is a strength, but don't let it prevent you from seeking help when needed
  • Regular reflection prevents emotional buildup and supports sustainable wellbeing

Bottom Line

Your feelings of overwhelm and insignificance are understandable responses to challenging circumstances, but they don't reflect the truth of your impact and value. By using the CBT framework to examine the thoughts and beliefs driving these feelings, you can begin to shift your emotional experience. Focus on establishing consistent routines, especially your sleep-wake schedule, before adding new changes. Practice boundaries that allow you to care without absorbing others' chaos. Your journaling practice is a powerful tool for processing emotions regularly rather than waiting for breaks. Remember: you are not alone in this work, and seeking support is a sign of wisdom, not weakness. The energy generated by your emotions can guide you toward meaningful action or necessary rest—learning to read this information is key to sustainable wellbeing.
