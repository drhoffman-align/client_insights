Communication in Family Dynamics

What you tend to do:

  • Default to understanding and accommodating
  • Present softer, more palatable versions of your feelings

🎯 The Core Problem: Minimizing Yourself to Accommodate Others

"You try to accept and understand, and that's your default. But even that isn't enough."

The Pattern Identified

What's happening: You minimize your own feelings and needs in favor of understanding and accommodating your partner's strong opinions and emotional reactions

How this shows up:

  • You feel hurt → but call it "annoyed" to sound less intense
  • You feel let down → but rationalize why it's "not that bad"
  • You feel resentful → but focus on understanding your partner's perspective
  • Your needs aren't met → but you prioritize keeping the peace
  • You're doing most of the parenting → but minimize the burden

The therapist's insight: "You just can't do right. So what do you think this should look like?"

Why This Pattern Exists

Your intention: To maintain harmony, avoid conflict, seem kind and understanding

What actually happens: This prevents authentic communication and resolution

The dynamic created:

You: Soften your feelings, accommodate, seek to understand

Partner: Expresses strong opinions and emotional reactions

Result: One-sided accommodation, not mutual respect

You feel: Unseen, frustrated, hurt — but don't say it clearly

Cycle continues: Nothing changes because your real feelings aren't communicated

The "Main Parent" Reality

The therapist's observation: "You're not just the default parent — you're the main parent."

What this means:

  • You carry the majority of parenting responsibilities
  • This isn't by choice — it's by default
  • Your partner is less consistently involved
  • You're often doing it alone
  • This creates feelings of being overwhelmed and unappreciated

The metaphor used: You "get the short end of the stick" — highlighting the imbalance

⚠️ Why Downplaying Emotions Is Costly

  • Prevents real connection: Your partner doesn't know how you truly feel
  • Blocks resolution: Problems can't be solved if they're minimized
  • Builds resentment: Unspoken hurt accumulates over time
  • Creates helplessness: "I just can't do right" mentality develops
  • Maintains imbalance: Unequal parenting load continues

🎨 Framework #1: The Feelings Wheel

"The thing about feelings — you can have multiple feelings, and I can see why 'annoyed' is a big one."

The Four-Step Process

1 Identify the Core Emotion

What this means: Start with the broad category of what you're feeling

Common core emotions:

  • Let down
  • Frustrated
  • Hurt
  • Angry
  • Sad
  • Scared

In your situation: When your partner isn't involved with parenting or dismisses your business idea, the core emotions might be "let down," "frustrated," or "hurt"

2 Use the Feelings Wheel to Drill Down to More Specific Emotions

What this means: Get more precise about exactly what you're feeling

Example progression:

Start broad: "I feel let down"

Get specific: Is it disappointment? Betrayal? Resentment?

Drill deeper: "I feel resentful that I'm doing most of the parenting alone"

Another example:

Start broad: "I feel frustrated"

Get specific: Is it annoyed? Irritated? Exasperated?

Drill deeper: "I feel annoyed that my partner reacts emotionally to my business idea instead of discussing it logically"

The therapist's observation: You often feel "let down" and "frustrated" but tend to label these as "annoyed" or "not a big deal" — but "resentful" might be more accurate

3 Reflect on the Context and Triggers

What this means: Understand what specifically triggered this emotion

Questions to ask:

  • What just happened?
  • What did my partner say or do (or not do)?
  • Why does this particular situation trigger resentment/hurt/frustration?
  • Is this a pattern or a one-time event?
  • What need of mine is not being met?

4 Validate the Full Intensity of the Feeling

What this means: Don't soften it — honor what you actually feel

Instead of downplaying:

❌ "I'm just a little annoyed, it's not a big deal"

❌ "I'm fine, really, I understand why he feels that way"

❌ "It's okay, I can handle the parenting alone"

Instead, validate fully:

✓ "I feel resentful that I'm carrying most of the parenting load"

✓ "I feel hurt that my partner dismissed my business idea without real discussion"

✓ "I feel let down that you weren't there to help again"

Why this matters: Naming emotions accurately helps you understand your needs and communicate them clearly, rather than defaulting to minimizing or rationalizing

✓ Benefits of Using the Feelings Wheel

Emotional clarity: You understand what you're actually feeling

Better communication: Specific words help others understand you

Self-validation: Honoring full intensity of feelings validates your experience

Needs identification: Precise emotions point to specific unmet needs

💪 Framework #2: Assertive Communication and Collaborative Decision-Making

"If we're making decisions for the family, then we should make family decisions."

The Four-Step Process

1 Clearly State Your Feelings and Needs Without Downplaying

What this means: Use your accurate emotion words from the Feelings Wheel

How to do this:

  • "I feel resentful that I'm doing most of the parenting alone"
  • "I feel hurt that you dismissed my idea without discussion"
  • "I need more support with our son"
  • "I need us to make this decision together, not just have you react"

The therapist's guidance: State it directly without minimizing — this is assertive, not aggressive

2 Invite Your Partner to Provide Their Reasoning and Logic

What this means: Ask for logical explanations, not just emotional reactions

Example with the Airbnb business:

Instead of accepting: "I just don't like it" or "It doesn't feel safe"

Ask for specifics: "What specific safety concerns do you have? Let's address each one logically"

Challenge: "I've researched this. Here's what I found. What logical reasons make you opposed?"

The therapist's challenge: "I challenge you: did you hear what I said? There's more to consider than just your feelings."

Applied to your partner: His emotional reactions matter, but so does logic and your perspective

3 Engage in Active Listening

What this means: Both partners fully hear and reflect back what the other says

How active listening works:

  • You speak: Share your feelings and needs clearly
  • Partner reflects: "What I hear you saying is..."
  • Partner speaks: Shares their perspective with logic
  • You reflect: "What I hear you saying is..."
  • Both clarify: "Is that accurate?" "Did I understand correctly?"

Why this matters: This breaks the cycle of black-and-white thinking and ensures both people feel heard

4 Make Decisions Together

What this means: Both perspectives are valued and considered

The therapist's principle: "If we're making decisions for the family, then we should make family decisions."

Collaborative decision-making process:

  1. Both partners state their perspective
  2. Both provide reasoning (emotional AND logical)
  3. Both listen and reflect back
  4. Identify areas of agreement
  5. Address areas of disagreement with data/logic
  6. Make decision together that honors both voices

The therapist's reminder: "Your opinion matters, but so does mine. Both are pretty strong."

✓ What This Framework Creates

Mutual respect: Both partners' voices matter equally

Shared responsibility: Not one-sided accommodation

Better decisions: Logic + emotion from both people

Authentic connection: Real feelings, not softened versions

📱 Framework #3: Addressing Avoidance

"Avoidance won't make it go away. The easiest way is to bring it up in a text message."

The Three-Step Process

1 Notice When You Are Avoiding a Difficult Conversation

Signs you're avoiding:

  • Hoping an issue will resolve itself
  • Feeling anxious about bringing something up
  • Telling yourself "it's not that important"
  • Waiting for the "right time" that never comes
  • Staying quiet to keep the peace

2 Use a Low-Stakes Method to Introduce the Topic

The therapist's suggestion: Send a text message

Why this works:

  • Lower barrier to entry than face-to-face
  • Gives you time to craft your message
  • Makes it harder to avoid (it's there in writing)
  • Gives both people time to prepare

Example text:

"Hey, I need to talk to you about the parenting schedule. I'm feeling overwhelmed being the main parent and need us to work together more. Can we set aside time tonight or tomorrow to discuss?"

3 Set a Time to Discuss It

What this means: Schedule the conversation so both people can prepare

Benefits of scheduling:

  • Both partners can think about the issue
  • Reduces emotional reactivity
  • Shows respect for both people's time
  • Creates commitment to have the conversation

Why this matters: Avoidance perpetuates unresolved issues. Proactive communication creates space for resolution and reduces anxiety.
