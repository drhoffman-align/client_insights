⏰ Framework: Time Blocking for Life Design

"Time blocking is a way to manage your energy and responsibilities and focus by assigning set times for the most important parts of your life."

The Seven-Step Process

1 Brainstorm All Recurring Responsibilities

What this means: List every regular activity in your week — work, meals, sleep, family time, chores, exercise, etc.

The therapist's instruction: "List all of the things that you need to do regularly. Just brainstorm on your day to day activities during your week."

Examples of what to include:

  • Basic needs: Sleep, meals, personal hygiene, getting ready
  • Work: Deep focus time, meetings, emails, admin tasks, business development
  • Family: Quality time with partner, kids' activities, family meals, conversations
  • Personal development: Reading, learning, reflection, goal planning
  • Health: Exercise, meal prep, doctor appointments, rest
  • Household: Cleaning, laundry, errands, maintenance
  • Social: Friends, community, connection
  • Miscellaneous: Hobbies, creative projects, downtime

Why this matters: Brainstorming ensures nothing important is forgotten

The key: Nothing is too small — include everything that takes your time and energy

2 Group Responsibilities into Distinct Areas of Life

What this means: Categorize each activity into clear, non-overlapping areas

The therapist's instruction: "Group them into areas of life. No overlaps. Just the areas clearly distinguished."

Example categories:

  • Basic Needs: Sleep, meals, hygiene
  • Work/Career: All professional responsibilities
  • Family: Partner, children, extended family time
  • Personal Development: Learning, growth, self-reflection
  • Health & Fitness: Exercise, wellness, medical care
  • Household Duties: Cleaning, maintenance, organization
  • Social/Community: Friends, networking, community involvement
  • Creative/Hobbies: Personal interests, passion projects
  • Miscellaneous: Everything else that doesn't fit above

Important: Avoid overlaps — each activity should fit clearly into ONE category

Why this matters: Grouping clarifies where your energy goes and prevents double-counting

3 Prioritize Areas of Life

What this means: Rank each area by importance to your well-being and goals

The therapist's instruction: "Prioritize them by what matters most. Think of Maslow's hierarchy of needs."

Maslow's Hierarchy as a Guide:

  1. Physiological: Sleep, food, water, shelter → Basic Needs (highest priority)
  2. Safety: Security, stability, health → Work, Health, Household
  3. Love/Belonging: Relationships, connection → Family, Social
  4. Esteem: Achievement, respect, growth → Personal Development, Career advancement
  5. Self-Actualization: Purpose, creativity, fulfillment → Creative projects, Hobbies

Example priority order:

  1. Basic Needs (sleep, meals, hygiene)
  2. Health & Fitness
  3. Work/Career (provides stability)
  4. Family
  5. Personal Development
  6. Household Duties
  7. Social/Community
  8. Creative/Hobbies
  9. Miscellaneous

Why this matters: Prioritizing aligns your schedule with your values and needs

The key: "The things that matter most are what you are going to chart first."

4 Assign Time Blocks

What this means: Plot each area's activities into specific time slots on your weekly schedule, starting with highest priorities

Example weekly structure:

  • 10:00 PM - 6:00 AM: Sleep (Basic Needs - Priority #1)
  • 6:00 - 7:00 AM: Morning routine, breakfast (Basic Needs)
  • 7:00 - 8:00 AM: Exercise (Health & Fitness - Priority #2)
  • 8:00 - 12:00 PM: Work - Deep Focus (Work/Career - Priority #3)
  • 12:00 - 1:00 PM: Lunch, rest (Basic Needs)
  • 1:00 - 5:00 PM: Work - Meetings, collaboration, admin
  • 5:00 - 6:00 PM: Personal Development (reading, planning)
  • 6:00 - 8:00 PM: Family Time (dinner, quality time)
  • 8:00 - 10:00 PM: Household, wind down, prep for bed

Why this matters: Assigning time blocks creates structure and predictability

The result: Your most important values are reflected in how you spend your time

5 Customize and Adjust

What this means: Not every activity needs to happen daily — alternate or rotate as needed, but keep the structure consistent

The therapist's guidance: "You don't have to do everything every day. You can interchange those time blocks for the same hours."

Examples of alternating blocks:

  • Exercise (7:00-8:00 AM):
    • Monday/Wednesday/Friday: Gym workout
    • Tuesday/Thursday: Yoga or walking
    • Saturday: Long outdoor activity
    • Sunday: Rest day
  • Personal Development (5:00-6:00 PM):
    • Monday/Wednesday: Reading business books
    • Tuesday/Thursday: Online course or skill development
    • Friday: Weekly planning and reflection
  • Household (varies):
    • Monday: Laundry
    • Tuesday: Deep clean kitchen
    • Wednesday: Errands
    • Thursday: Meal prep
    • Saturday: Major cleaning

The flexibility: Activities within blocks can vary, but the TIME remains consistent

Why this matters: Customization allows flexibility without losing consistency

6 Reduce Distractions

What this means: Within each time block (especially work), further break down tasks and limit interruptions

The therapist's instruction: "The goal is to decrease distractions."

Strategies for work blocks:

  • Email batching: Check email only 2-3 times per day (e.g., 9 AM, 1 PM, 4 PM)
  • Phone on silent: During deep focus blocks
  • Sub-blocks within work:
    • 8:00-10:00 AM: Strategic planning
    • 10:00-12:00 PM: Client work or projects
    • 1:00-3:00 PM: Meetings
    • 3:00-4:00 PM: Admin and email
    • 4:00-5:00 PM: Business development
  • Close unnecessary tabs/apps: Only what's needed for current task
  • Use timers: Pomodoro technique or similar

Why this matters: Reducing distractions increases focus and productivity

The result: You accomplish more in less time, with less stress

7 Review and Refine

What this means: Try the schedule, observe what works, and adjust as needed

The therapist's approach: "After you create this time block schedule, we'll work it out and see how it goes."

Questions to ask after one week:

  • What worked well?
  • What felt too rigid or unrealistic?
  • Which areas got consistent attention?
  • Which areas still felt neglected?
  • Did you have enough transition time between blocks?
  • Were the time allocations appropriate for each area?
  • What unexpected challenges came up?
  • How did you feel overall — more or less stressed?

Why this matters: Reviewing ensures the system evolves with your life

Remember: This is about progress, not perfection — aim for consistency, not rigidity

🛠️ Practical Instructions: Creating Your Time-Blocked Week

What to Do

✓ Your Step-by-Step Action Plan

  1. List all your recurring weekly activities
    • Include everything: work, sleep, meals, family, exercise, chores
    • Nothing is too small
  2. Group them into clear, non-overlapping areas of life
    • Basic Needs, Work, Family, Health, Personal Development, etc.
    • Each activity fits into ONE category only
  3. Rank these areas by importance
    • Use Maslow's hierarchy as a guide
    • What must happen first for other areas to succeed?
  4. Assign each activity to a specific time block
    • Start with highest priorities (sleep, meals, work, family)
    • Be realistic about time needed
    • Include transition time
  5. Alternate or rotate activities that don't need daily attention
    • Keep the time block consistent
    • Vary what happens within that time
  6. Within work blocks, further break down tasks
    • Limit distractions
    • Batch similar activities (email, calls, admin)
    • Check email only 2-3 times per day at set times
  7. Review your schedule after one week
    • What worked? What didn't?
    • Adjust as needed

When to Use Time Blocking

Best Times to Apply This Framework

  • At the start of each week — Sunday evening or Monday morning planning
  • When feeling overwhelmed or scattered — Return to your structure
  • When noticing important areas are being neglected — Family time, health, personal growth
  • During life transitions — New job, new family situation, new responsibilities
  • When preparing for busy seasons — Plan ahead to maintain balance

What to Avoid

⚠️ Common Pitfalls

  • Overlapping categories
    • Don't put same activity in multiple areas
    • Keep categories distinct
  • Trying to do everything every day
    • Alternate activities within time blocks
    • Some things only need attention 2-3 times per week
  • Letting urgent but unimportant tasks take over
    • Stick to your schedule
    • Non-emergencies can wait for their designated time
  • Being too rigid
    • Aim for consistency, not perfection
    • Allow flexibility when truly needed
  • Neglecting transition time
    • Build in buffer between major blocks
    • Don't schedule back-to-back all day

📚 Clinical Concepts Explained

Time Blocking

Assigning specific periods in your calendar to focus on particular tasks or areas of life, rather than multitasking or reacting to interruptions.

In your life: Instead of letting your day happen to you, you intentionally structure it so important areas get consistent attention.

Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs

A model that ranks human needs from basic (physiological, safety) to higher-level (love/belonging, esteem, self-actualization).

In your life: Used here to prioritize life areas — you can't focus on personal growth if you're not sleeping or eating well.

The order:

  1. Physiological (sleep, food, water)
  2. Safety (security, health, stability)
  3. Love/Belonging (family, relationships)
  4. Esteem (achievement, growth)
  5. Self-Actualization (purpose, creativity)

Prioritization

Deciding what matters most and giving it the most time and energy.

In your life: Sleep and meals come before hobbies; family time is protected, not leftover time.

Consistency vs. Reactivity

Consistency: Acting according to a plan, ensuring priorities get regular attention

Reactivity: Responding to whatever comes up, often at the expense of your priorities

In your life: The difference between family time happening every evening (consistent) vs. only when there's nothing urgent at work (reactive)
