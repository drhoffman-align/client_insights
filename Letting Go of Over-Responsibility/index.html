🔄 Framework #1: Reframing Self-Talk

"Failure is not what you're doing."

The Four-Step Process

1 Notice Harsh, Punitive Language

What to catch: When you say "I failed"

Your pattern: You often call yourself a failure when you don't meet goals, even when circumstances (like losing your job or custody) were out of your control

2 Pause and Examine the Situation for External Factors

Ask yourself: What was really in my control?

Look for: Circumstances, other people's actions, systemic factors

The broken bike metaphor: If the bike breaks, it's not your failure — you just didn't have the means to reach the goal

3 Replace "Failure" Language with More Accurate, Compassionate Descriptions

Instead of: "I failed"

Try: "I faced difficult circumstances"

The therapist's insight: "Words like failure... is like somebody else's success."

Important distinction: "There is a difference between toxic positivity and reframing."

Reframing is about telling yourself the truth, not just "thinking positive"

4 Tell Yourself the Truth About What Happened

Include: What was and wasn't in your control

Be accurate: Not overly positive, not overly harsh — just truthful

✓ Why This Framework Matters

Harsh self-talk reinforces negative self-concepts and perfectionism

Leads to emotional pain and burnout

Reframing helps align self-perception with reality

Reduces unnecessary guilt and fosters self-compassion


The logic: Words shape beliefs and emotions. By changing the narrative from "I failed" to "I faced difficult circumstances," you reduce self-punishment and open space for healing.

Understanding Negative Reinforcement

What you're doing: Using harsh self-talk or punishment to motivate yourself

Short-term: May work to push you forward

Long-term: Causes harm, exhaustion, burnout

The therapist's reminder: "You don't necessarily need to punish yourself to feel better. You can actually just feel better."

⚖️ Framework #2: Differentiating Victimhood from Victim Mentality

"Being a victim and having a victim mentality are two different things."

The Four-Step Process

1 Recognize When You Have Been Harmed or Targeted by Others

The reality: Anyone can be a victim of others' actions

This is about what happened TO you

2 Allow Yourself to Acknowledge Being a Victim Without Equating It to Helplessness

What you fear: That being a victim means you're weak or helpless

The truth: Being a victim is about what happened to you, not who you are

Example from your life: Losing your daughters was due to someone else's actions, not your own failure

3 Distinguish Between Being a Victim and Having a Victim Mentality

Being a victim: An event — experiencing harm

Victim mentality: A persistent worldview — believing you are always powerless

Key difference: One is something that happened; the other is a way of thinking

4 Accept That Acknowledging Harm Does Not Remove Your Agency

You can be both: A victim of someone's actions AND someone with strength and agency

These are not mutually exclusive

✓ Why This Framework Matters

Avoiding the label of "victim" leads to taking on too much responsibility

Suppresses valid emotions

Accepting victimhood when appropriate allows for emotional processing and boundary-setting


The logic: Denying this truth leads to misplaced responsibility and emotional stagnation.

🔄 Framework #3: Returning Agency to Others

"You take on everybody's agency."

The Four-Step Process

1 Identify Responsibilities You Have Taken On That Belong to Others

Your pattern: "Autocorrecting" for other people's mistakes

What this looks like: Fixing things for others, taking on their consequences, absorbing all blame and effort

2 Consciously "Give Back" Agency to Those Responsible for Their Actions

What this means: Let others own their actions

Rather than: Absorbing all blame and effort yourself

3 Set Boundaries to Prevent Further Overextension

Practice: Saying no or limiting your involvement when others expect you to "fix" things

Remember: Setting boundaries is self-care, not selfishness

4 Monitor Your Emotional Response as You Shift Responsibility

Notice: How it feels to let others handle their own consequences

You may feel: Guilt, anxiety, relief — all are normal

✓ Why This Framework Matters

Overextending yourself leads to exhaustion and resentment

Returning agency helps restore balance

Protects your well-being


The logic: You cannot control everything; others must be accountable for their own actions and consequences.

💔 Framework #4: Emotional Identification and Expression

"You deserve a lot more emotions... a lot of yours have been muted or dampened."

The Four-Step Process

1 List and Name Your Emotions

Emotions to identify: Anger, shame, grief, regret, disappointment, sadness, fear

Be specific: Don't just say "bad" — name the actual emotion

2 Notice Which Emotions Are "Muted" or Suppressed

Your pattern: Emotions are often "muted" or "dampened"

What gets suppressed: Especially anger, disappointment, and grief

3 Allow Yourself to Feel and Express Emotions in Healthy Ways

Healthy expression looks like:

  • Stating your anger directly
  • Journaling
  • Talking to trusted people
  • Setting boundaries based on feelings

4 Avoid Bottling Up Emotions

The warning: Bottling up "feeds the beast" and leads to emotional eruptions

What happens: Suppressed emotions build up and result in uncontrollable outbursts

✓ Why This Framework Matters

Suppressing emotions leads to internal distress

Can result in uncontrollable outbursts

Healthy expression allows for emotional processing and resolution


The logic: Emotions need to "cycle through" to prevent buildup and negative consequences.
