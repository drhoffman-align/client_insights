💪 Reclaiming Power and Purpose

Learning to Put Yourself First

📍 The Situation: The Cost of Always Putting Others First

"If you're constantly working for everybody else, then how do you create stability for yourself?"

Where You Are Now

Current status: Navigating the job market after a period of unemployment

What you're feeling: Weighed down by pressure to secure a position that aligns with your skills and values

Your Longstanding Pattern

What you do: Consistently put others' needs before your own

How this shows up: In professional and personal contexts, often at the expense of your own well-being and stability

How long: This pattern has been present for a long time

Sometimes it feels: Rewarding

But it also results in:

  • Emotional pain
  • Lack of recognition
  • Being taken advantage of by others
  • Instability
  • Burnout
  • Lack of fulfillment

How You Handle Your Own Pain

What you do: Struggle to process your own hurt

Your tendency: Skip over your feelings, focusing instead on helping others or moving on to the next task

The therapist's observation: "Missing your feelings misses a lot of information."

What Happens to Your Contributions

The pattern: You generate ideas or put in effort, but others take credit or benefit more than you do

Examples:

  • Made others money or advanced their goals
  • Haven't received recognition or stability in return
  • "Leaving your mark" but having someone else claim it as theirs

Your strength: "Multipurpose like Windex" — your versatility is impressive

The problem: This versatility should benefit you, not just others

The core problem: Chronic self-sacrifice and over-empathy, which leads to giving away your power and not advocating for your own needs or interests.

The result: Others benefit from your ideas and efforts without reciprocation or acknowledgment, leaving you feeling drained, unappreciated, and sometimes exploited.

The mismatch: Your intentions (to help and be needed) vs. reality that your contributions are often overlooked or appropriated by others, which undermines your sense of purpose and self-worth.

"Your empathy is outstanding. But it is — it's over the top."

⚡ The Power-Purpose Alignment Framework

"You should be able to actually control your purpose, your output, instead of letting others do that for you."

The Six-Step Process

1 Notice Your Pattern

What to recognize: When you are prioritizing others at your own expense

Signs you're giving away your power:

  • Offering ideas without ensuring credit
  • Working hard for others' benefit with no return
  • Excusing harmful behavior because of empathy
  • Skipping over your own pain to help someone else

The metaphor: Wearing "rose colored glasses" — ignoring others' negative behavior

Another metaphor: "Shopping spree" or "big giveaways" — giving away major things (ideas, effort, power) without return

Why this matters: Noticing the pattern is the first step to change

2 Process Your Feelings

What to do: Allow yourself to feel and reflect on your own pain, hurt, or disappointment instead of skipping over it

Pause and ask: "What am I feeling right now?"

Then: Allow yourself to sit with any pain, disappointment, or frustration. Don't rush to fix or help someone else.

The therapist's guidance: "Missing your feelings misses a lot of information."

Why this matters: Processing feelings prevents emotional buildup and provides valuable insight

3 Extract Information

What to do: Use your feelings as data to understand what happened and what you need

Reflect on: What this feeling is telling you about your needs or boundaries

Questions to ask:

  • What boundary was crossed?
  • What did I need that I didn't get?
  • What does this tell me about this relationship or situation?

Why this matters: Extracting information helps you learn from experience

4 Strategic Adaptation

What to do: Apply what you've learned to future situations, making adjustments to protect your interests

Decide: What you want to do differently next time

Examples:

  • Speak up for yourself
  • Ask for recognition
  • Say no
  • Set clearer expectations upfront

The therapist's insight: "Adaptability doesn't happen by ignoring things. Adaptability happens by taking notice."

Why this matters: Strategic adaptation increases resilience and prevents repeated exploitation

5 Assert Ownership

What to do: Ensure your contributions are recognized and that you retain control over your ideas and efforts

When contributing ideas or effort: Make sure your role is visible and acknowledged

The therapist's guidance: "You have the same power that you've always had, which is to walk into a place and give away your ideas."

But: You need to assert ownership instead of letting others claim your work

Why this matters: Asserting ownership builds self-worth and credibility

6 Align Actions with Purpose

What to do: Make sure what you do serves both your own goals and, when appropriate, others' needs

The key insight: "Your purpose is just as strong as your power."

The shift: Your purpose should serve you, not just others

The goal: Not to stop helping others, but to ensure your efforts also serve your own growth, stability, and fulfillment

Why this matters: Aligning actions with purpose ensures fulfillment and sustainability

✓ The Logic Behind This Framework

Ignoring your own needs leads to burnout and being taken advantage of

By processing your emotions and learning from them, you can adapt, protect yourself, and ensure your efforts benefit you as well as others


"The more power you have, the more you can do with that power."

📊 Before vs. After

Before: You help others, skip over your own pain, and end up feeling used or invisible

After (desired): You process your feelings, learn from them, and set boundaries so your efforts are recognized and serve your own goals

🔍 Understanding Key Concepts

Empathy

What it is: The ability to understand and share the feelings of others

In your case: Your empathy is "over the top," leading to self-neglect

The balance needed: Empathy is a strength, but over-empathy can be self-destructive

Boundaries

What they are: Limits you set to protect your well-being

Your current situation: Lack of boundaries allows others to take advantage

What you need: Boundaries protect you from being exploited or overlooked

Ownership

What it means: Taking credit and responsibility for your own ideas and contributions

Why it's crucial: For recognition and self-worth

Adaptability

What true adaptability is: The ability to learn from experience and adjust behavior

Important: True adaptability requires processing emotions, not ignoring them

"Adaptability doesn't happen by ignoring things. Adaptability happens by taking notice."

Accountability

What it means: Expecting others to take responsibility for their actions

What you need to stop doing: Giving people "passes" because of their past

The therapist's guidance: "People actually do at some point have a choice. And so if they choose to be fucked up, it's their choice."

The shift needed: "Changing your behavior when it comes down to that — not giving passes — is very important."

Why this matters: Others' pasts do not excuse ongoing harmful behavior

🛠️ Practical Instructions

Step 1: Notice When You're Prioritizing Others

Pause and ask: "What am I feeling right now?"

Step 2: Sit with Your Feelings

Allow yourself to experience any pain, disappointment, or frustration. Don't rush to fix or help someone else.

Step 3: Reflect on What Your Feelings Tell You

What do they say about your needs or boundaries?

Step 4: Decide What to Do Differently Next Time

Examples: Speak up for yourself, ask for recognition, say no

Step 5: Ensure Your Contributions Are Visible

When contributing ideas or effort, make sure your role is acknowledged

Step 6: Hold Others Accountable

If someone mistreats you, hold them accountable rather than excusing their behavior because of empathy

What to Avoid

  • Skipping over your feelings
  • Giving away credit
  • Excusing harmful behavior
  • Working for everyone else without return

Being Selective with Your Energy

Remember: Not everyone deserves your energy or ideas — be selective
