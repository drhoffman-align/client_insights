📅 Framework #1: Time Block Scheduling

"Time block scheduling is different than time blocking. It's about attributing consistent time to each area of your life."

The Four-Step Process

1 Identify Key Areas of Life

Examples of life areas: Work, family, self-care, business growth, fitness, parenting, caregiver responsibilities

For you specifically:

  • CEO tasks and daily operations
  • Business development and growth
  • Parenting time (ages 11 and 6)
  • Time with mother (dementia care)
  • Personal health and fitness
  • Relationship/partner time
  • Self-reflection and planning

2 Allocate Consistent, Dedicated Blocks

Key word: CONSISTENT - Not sporadic, but regular scheduled times

Example blocks for your week:

  • Monday 9-11am: Deep work on business strategy
  • Tuesday/Thursday 6-7:30pm: Family dinner and homework help
  • Wednesday morning: Email processing block (avoid constant interruptions)
  • Friday afternoon: Business development calls
  • Saturday morning: Time with mother
  • Sunday evening: Weekly planning and journaling

What makes it "scheduling" vs "blocking": Assigning specific, recurring times to life domains rather than reactively filling calendar

3 Maintain These Blocks Regularly

The key: Rather than sporadically addressing areas when they become urgent, keep the structure predictable

This prevents:

  • Tasks "falling off" your radar
  • Last-minute scrambles
  • Neglecting important areas until crisis hits
  • Constant multitasking and context-switching

Therapist's guidance: "Don't stop what you're doing. Keep going until we work on something else."

Practical tip: During each block, focus ONLY on that area - no multitasking

4 Adjust as Needed, But Keep Structure

Flexibility: Life happens - blocks can shift when emergencies arise

But: Maintain the overall structure and consistency - if you skip Monday's deep work, reschedule it for Tuesday

Use tools:

  • Reminders or alarms to transition between blocks
  • Calendar color-coding for different life areas
  • Weekly review to assess if blocks are working
Why this framework matters: Time block scheduling creates consistency, reduces decision fatigue, ensures all important areas receive attention.

Prevents: Tasks from "falling off" and supports better work-life balance - especially critical when managing CEO role, parenting, and caregiving simultaneously.

Especially helpful for ADHD: Where impulsivity and distractibility can derail plans - structure provides guardrails and reduces cognitive load of constant decision-making.

For you specifically: By assigning time to each domain, you avoid neglecting personal or family needs for business demands, and vice versa. Your day is packed - this ensures nothing critical gets dropped.

Example application: Setting aside dedicated time for emails prevents constant interruptions throughout the day, allowing deeper focus during other blocks.

💊 Framework #2: Medication Adjustment and Monitoring

"The goal is for you to feel what it's like to be truly stimulated."

The Four-Step Process

1 Assess Current Medication Effectiveness

Current medication: Vyvanse 10mg (long-acting stimulant)

What's working: Helps with focus

What's not optimal: May not be at optimal dose for task initiation and sustained motivation

Side effects noted: Increased sweating, some anxiety

Assessment: Current dose may be too low to fully address ADHD symptoms

2 Introduce Trial Medication (Adderall 10mg Short-Acting)

Why short-acting: Provides immediate feedback on effectiveness and side effects

Dosing: 10mg taken in the morning

Flexibility: "If the Adderall is too intense, you can always break it in half"

Safety considerations:

  • Do NOT take for first time on travel days
  • Do NOT take when you'll be sitting for long periods
  • Take on a regular morning when you can monitor effects
  • Wait 4-6 hours; if needed, can take Vyvanse after

3 Monitor Response, Side Effects, and Compare

What to track:

  • Focus and concentration levels
  • Task initiation (getting started on work)
  • Sustained motivation throughout day
  • Energy levels
  • Side effects: jitteriness, shakiness, anxiety, dry mouth, sweating
  • Appetite changes
  • Sleep quality

Compare: How does Adderall feel compared to Vyvanse? Better focus? More/less anxiety? Different energy pattern?

4 Adjust Dosage or Switch Medications Based on Feedback

Possible outcomes:

  • Adderall works better → Consider switching
  • Vyvanse works better → Consider increasing Vyvanse dose
  • Both work similarly → Discuss preference and convenience
  • Side effects too strong → Can reduce dose, try different medication

Therapist's reassurance: "You can stop medications early on anytime if you need to" - stimulants clear quickly from system

Goal: Find optimal medication and dose for focus, motivation, task completion with minimal side effects

Why this framework matters: Finding the right medication and dose can significantly improve focus, motivation, and daily functioning for ADHD.

Logic: Short-acting stimulants provide immediate feedback on effectiveness and side effects, helping to fine-tune treatment before committing to long-acting regimen.

Your current situation: Vyvanse 10mg helps but may not be optimal dose - trial helps determine if different stimulant or higher dose would be more effective.

Safety first: Taking trial on regular day (not travel) allows you to monitor effects and make adjustments as needed without risk.

Morning vs. night: Morning journaling leverages your clearest thinking time and sets intentional tone for day. Night journaling can help wind down but morning is optimal for planning and focus.

For you: Already have journaling practice - simply shifting timing to morning will enhance benefits by using "best brain" time for yourself.

👵 Framework #4: Dementia Care Guidance

"If you can get help for your mom sooner, while she's still comfortable, it will help in the long run."

The Four-Step Process

1 Recognize Progressive Nature and Importance of Early Intervention

Understanding dementia:

  • Progressive decline in cognitive function, memory, and behavior
  • "Sundowning" - increased confusion and agitation in evening
  • Behavioral changes including paranoia, aggression, wandering
  • Difficulty with daily activities increases over time

Therapist's emphasis: "Dementia is not something that should be taken lightly. Early, stable care is best."

2 Seek Stable, Consistent Care Environments

Why stability matters: Frequent changes increase confusion and distress for dementia patients

What to look for:

  • Memory care facilities with specialized dementia support
  • Environments that offer activities and social engagement
  • Familiar routines and consistent caregivers
  • Safety features (secure building, monitoring)
  • Staff trained in dementia care

Timing: Better to transition while mother is still comfortable rather than waiting for crisis

3 Prepare for Behavioral Changes

Common changes to expect:

  • Sundowning - confusion/agitation in evening
  • Paranoia or accusations
  • Repetitive questions or behaviors
  • Difficulty recognizing family members
  • Wandering or getting lost
  • Changes in sleep patterns

How to respond: Remain calm, redirect gently, don't argue about reality, maintain routines

4 Access Resources and Support Early

Resources to explore:

  • Alzheimer's Association (support groups, education)
  • Adult day care programs
  • In-home care services
  • Memory care facilities
  • Respite care for family caregivers
  • Legal/financial planning (power of attorney, etc.)

Involve your sister in planning - shared caregiving reduces burden and provides support

Therapist's guidance: Proactive planning supports better outcomes for both patient and family

Why this framework matters: Early, consistent care improves comfort and safety for dementia patients and reduces caregiver stress.

Logic: Delaying care or frequent changes can increase confusion and distress for those with dementia. Proactive planning supports better outcomes for both patient and family.

Your situation: Mother's dementia is progressing - seeking stable care sooner rather than later will maintain her comfort and safety while reducing future distress.

Key takeaway: This is not abandonment - it's ensuring best possible care while maintaining your own capacity to be present and supportive.

📚 Clinical Concepts Explained

ADHD (Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder)

A neurodevelopmental disorder characterized by inattention, hyperactivity, and impulsivity. In adults, it often presents as distractibility, forgetfulness, and difficulty with organization.

Stimulant Medications

Medications like Vyvanse and Adderall increase dopamine and norepinephrine in the brain, improving focus and executive function. Short-acting forms provide immediate effects; long-acting forms offer sustained coverage throughout day.

Dementia

A progressive decline in cognitive function, memory, and behavior. "Sundowning" refers to increased confusion and agitation in the evening hours.

Time Block Scheduling

A method of structuring the day by assigning fixed periods to specific activities or domains, rather than multitasking or reacting to demands as they arise. Different from simple "time blocking" - it's about consistent attribution of time to life areas.

Journaling

A self-reflective practice that helps process emotions, clarify thoughts, and set goals. Morning journaling particularly effective for leveraging clearest thinking time.
