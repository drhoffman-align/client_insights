Therapeutic Insights

Alignment Psychiatry

The Situation

You are a teacher processing multiple traumatic events: a violent classroom incident involving a student with autism who brought scissors to school, confusing and seemingly unjust administrative responses including questionable suspensions, and the recent murder of a former student. You feel unsupported by school leadership, suspect systemic racism and cover-ups, and are struggling with intense emotions including anger, grief, disappointment, and confusion. Old coping patterns like binge eating and emotional numbness have resurfaced as you navigate these overwhelming circumstances while trying to maintain job security.

Core Problem

You struggle to process and express overwhelming emotions without turning them inward through self-harm, binge eating, or emotional suppression, or outward through impulsive actions that could jeopardize your employment. You experience cycles of self-blame and powerlessness despite many factors being outside your control.

"Your emotions are valid signals requiring attention, not enemies to suppress."

Clinical Concepts

Borderline Personality Disorder

Condition marked by emotional extremes, black-and-white thinking, and difficulty regulating feelings; manifests through swings between numbness and overwhelming emotion.

Emotional Suppression

Pushing feelings down rather than processing them, leading to numbness or acting out behaviors.

Net Positive/Negative Emotional Energy

Some emotions (anger, anxiety) create energy driving action; others (depression, grief) drain energy causing withdrawal.

Strategic Action

Acting with intention to protect oneself and others rather than reacting impulsively or self-destructively.

Key Insight: Suppressed emotions emerge through unhealthy outlets like binge eating or numbness. Learning to channel emotional energy strategically protects both your wellbeing and your livelihood.

Practical Instructions

When you feel overwhelmed by emotions:

  • Pause and identify what you're feeling (anger, grief, confusion, disappointment)
  • Ask yourself: "Does this emotion give me energy or drain me?"
  • If it gives energy (anger, anxiety), channel it into physical activity like gardening or strategic action like documentation
  • If it drains energy (grief, depression), allow yourself rest and self-compassion
  • Notice black-and-white thoughts ("I must fix everything" or "I'm powerless") and seek the gray area

When facing workplace injustice:

  • Gather information first—listen to students without making promises
  • Document everything you observe and hear
  • Empower students to share their accounts with parents or trusted adults
  • Use collective voices rather than solo confrontation
  • Protect your job security by acting strategically, not impulsively

When old patterns resurface:

  • Recognize binge eating or numbness as signals that emotions need processing
  • Journal about what you're feeling and what energy you notice
  • Choose one small strategic action instead of suppressing or acting out
  • Remember: you're not responsible for everything—don't accept misplaced blame
Remember: Strategic advocacy uses collective voices and documentation rather than solo confrontation. You can make a difference while protecting yourself.

Homework

  • Ask returning students to write what happened in their own words
  • Encourage students to share accounts with parents or trusted adults without promising outcomes
  • Journal your emotional responses after conversations—what feelings arise, what energy you notice, how you choose to act
  • Continue gardening or physical activities to process emotional energy
  • Document events and gather evidence before taking action
