Therapeutic Insights

Alignment Psychiatry

The Situation

You are recovering from a destabilizing experience in a dysfunctional nonprofit workplace that eroded your self-confidence and professional identity. Currently not working, you are struggling with disrupted sleep patterns—falling asleep after 3 a.m. and waking in the afternoon—along with a lack of daily structure and questions about your PR career direction. You continue to experience anxiety, OCD symptoms, and circadian rhythm disruption despite your current medication regimen.

Core Problem

The loss of structure and self-confidence stemming from a toxic workplace has been compounded by difficulty distinguishing between personal responsibility and systemic dysfunction. This has resulted in procrastination, sleep disruption, isolation, and misplaced self-blame for problems that predated your arrival and were beyond your control.

"The workplace dysfunction was systemic, not a personal failure. Focus on what you can control, not systemic problems."

Clinical Concepts

Circadian Rhythm

Your body's internal clock regulating sleep/wake cycles. Resetting this rhythm improves mood and overall functioning, creating a foundation for recovery.

Procrastination as Defense

Avoiding difficult emotional processing by focusing on low-stakes tasks. This protective mechanism keeps you busy while preventing deeper healing work.

Personal Development Time

Protected time for self-reflection and growth before daily demands take over. This ensures you give your best mental energy to yourself first.

Medication Titration

Gradually adjusting medication doses to balance therapeutic benefits with side effects, optimizing your treatment response.

Therapeutic Frameworks

Area-of-Life Time Blocking Framework
1
Identify all important life areas: Physical health, mental health, career, finance, purpose, self-improvement, spirituality, household, recreation, social connections, sleep, and eating.
2
Set ideal wake/sleep times: Establish consistent times that support healthy circadian rhythm and allow for morning personal development.
3
Block time for each area on weekly schedule: Create visual representation of how your week will be structured across all life domains.
4
Assign consistent time blocks: Designate specific recurring times for each life area to build routine and predictability.
5
Substitute activities within blocks: Maintain the structure even when changing specific activities—flexibility within consistency.
6
Incorporate outside-the-house activities: Break isolation by including activities that get you out into the world regularly.
Structure combats depression and restores a sense of control. Consistency matters more than perfection.
Medication-Assisted Circadian Reset
1
Set specific start date: Choose a date at least one week out to mentally prepare and increase commitment to the change.
2
Use medications to enforce sleep/wake times: Leverage medication timing to support your new schedule rather than fighting against it.
3
Adjust medication timing for optimal benefit: Take medications at times that align with and reinforce your desired sleep/wake cycle.
4
Track effects and adjust as needed: Monitor how medications affect your sleep, energy, and functioning, communicating observations for fine-tuning.
"Sleep and eating are foundational to all other functioning. Give your best mental energy to yourself first each day."

Practical Instructions

Start each day with 1-2 hours of personal development time. This is your time for self-reflection, journaling, and growth before the world makes demands on you.

Mentally rehearse your new sleep schedule nightly. Visualization increases commitment and prepares your mind for the transition.

Include outside-the-house activities regularly. Breaking isolation supports recovery and provides structure beyond your home environment.

Process work experiences through journaling. Writing helps distinguish between personal responsibility and systemic dysfunction, reducing misplaced self-blame.

Medication Adjustments

Adderall

Increase to 30-40 mg in the morning (from 20 mg) for improved focus and alertness throughout the day.

Fluvoxamine

Increase to 100 mg daily for OCD symptoms. Take at night if it causes drowsiness, using this side effect to support your sleep schedule.

Clonazepam

Take the full 2 mg at bedtime (not split) to induce sleep and help reset your circadian rhythm.

Monitor medication effects and side effects carefully. Communicate your observations so adjustments can be made as needed.

Homework

  • Set a specific date (at least one week out) to begin your new sleep schedule and mentally rehearse it nightly
  • Create a weekly time-blocked schedule covering all life areas: physical, mental, career, finance, purpose, self-improvement, spirituality, household, recreation, social, sleep, and eating
  • Start each day with 1-2 hours of personal development time
  • Track medication effects and side effects
  • Include outside-the-house activities to break isolation patterns

Key Takeaways

  • The workplace dysfunction was systemic, not a personal failure on your part
  • Structure combats depression and restores a sense of control over your life
  • Give your best mental energy to yourself first each day
  • Setting a start date for your sleep schedule increases commitment and follow-through
  • Maintain time-block structure even when substituting activities—flexibility within consistency
  • Focus on what you can control, not systemic problems beyond your influence
  • Consistency matters more than perfection in building new habits
  • Breaking isolation through outside activities supports recovery
  • Sleep and eating are foundational to all other areas of functioning
  • Process work experiences through journaling to gain clarity and perspective
  • Monitor medication effects and adjust as directed
  • Practice self-compassion and prioritize progress over perfection
Bottom Line: You are rebuilding from a workplace experience that was systemically dysfunctional, not a reflection of your worth or capabilities. By resetting your circadian rhythm, creating structured time blocks across all life areas, and giving yourself the gift of personal development time each morning, you are reclaiming control and laying the foundation for sustainable recovery. Structure, self-compassion, and consistency will guide you forward.
