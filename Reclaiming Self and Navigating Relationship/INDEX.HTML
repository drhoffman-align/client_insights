🔍 Understanding Key Concepts

Major Depression

What it is: A mood disorder characterized by persistent sadness, loss of interest, and impaired daily functioning

For you: This manifests as emotional exhaustion and difficulty finding motivation

Adjustment Disorder with Anxiety

What it is: Emotional or behavioral symptoms in response to a significant life change or stressor

For you: Your anxiety is heightened by work stress and marital conflict

Emotional Safety

What it is: The ability to express feelings without fear of negative consequences

For you: You lack this in your marriage, leading to emotional withdrawal

Accountability

What it is: Taking responsibility for one's actions and their impact

Important note: You may be "too accountable," taking on more blame than is warranted

Moving the Goalpost

What it is: A dynamic where the criteria for resolution keep changing, preventing closure

The effect: Keeps you in a state of uncertainty and frustration

"You end up with all these unresolved things because every time there's an issue or problem to address, there's always another problem that comes up — kind of moving the goalpost."

✍️ Framework #2: Written Communication for Clarity

"Written form is usually better because you can explain yourself, your why, and give examples or evidence."

The Five-Step Process

1 Write a Letter

What to do: Express your thoughts, needs, and experiences in writing

Why: Bypasses immediate defensiveness

2 Include Evidence

What to do: Use concrete examples to illustrate your points

Why: Creates a record of your efforts and clarifies your position

3 Clarify Expectations

What to state:

  • What you have done
  • What you need
  • What you expect from your spouse

4 Describe Impact

What to explain: How unmet needs have affected you and the relationship

5 Invite Accountability

What to ask for: Openness, empathy, and accountability in response

Why this framework matters:

Written communication bypasses immediate defensiveness — allows both parties to reflect before responding

Creates a record of your efforts and clarifies your position

Reduces the chance of immediate emotional escalation

Because verbal attempts often lead to defensiveness, this allows you to express yourself fully and clearly

The Letter Rubric: What to Include

  1. What you've done for your spouse
  2. What you've done for yourself
  3. What you're aiming for
  4. What you expect from a spouse
  5. What you haven't received
  6. How things might have been different
  7. What you need to trust again
  8. What you need to see change

Focus on: Clarity, not blame

📊 Framework #3: Needs and Boundaries Inventory

"Nobody ever teaches people how to just stop, look, listen. This is what you got. This is what's needed, and this is how you move forward."

The Four-Step Process

1 List What You Provide

Identify: The ways you support your spouse

Examples: Financial stability, chores, emotional support

For you: You provide stability, freedom, and support in the relationship

2 List What You Need

Clarify: Your baseline needs

Examples: Empathy, nurturing, safety to express emotions

For you: Your needs for empathy and nurturing feel unmet

3 Identify Gaps

Note: Where your needs are unmet or where your efforts are unrecognized

For you: By listing these out, you can see the imbalance

4 Set Boundaries

Decide: What you will and will not accept moving forward

For you: Decide what changes are necessary for your well-being

Why this framework matters:

Helps you see the relationship more objectively

Recognize patterns and make informed decisions about next steps

Both partners need to meet each other's basic emotional needs for a relationship to thrive

Boundaries are necessary when your needs are consistently unmet

🛠️ Practical Instructions

During Medical Leave

  • Allow yourself to rest and "get your bearings" in the first month
  • Use the second month to rebuild routines and reconnect with your identity
  • In the third month, start planning for the future, whether that's a new job or a new life structure

For Relationship Communication

  • Write a detailed letter to your spouse using the provided rubric
  • Include what you've done, what you need, and how unmet needs have affected you
  • Ask for specific changes and accountability
  • Avoid verbal confrontations that escalate defensiveness
  • Use concrete examples and evidence when expressing your needs

Boundaries

  • Clearly define what you will and will not accept in the relationship
  • If your needs continue to go unmet, consider whether staying is in your best interest

⚠️ Important Reminders

Avoid "all or none" thinking — most situations are more nuanced

"All or none things are hardly ever true."

"When people use absolutes, to me, it is not the truth, but also it is a heightened sense of the truth."
