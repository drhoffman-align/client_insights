🔓 Framework #1: Acceptance and Transparency

"The best thing about transparency if you do it right, is that you can take accountability and you can provide understanding."

The Five-Step Framework

1 Accept That Some Marks Will Follow You

What this means: Accept that some negative marks or incidents will be on your record and will require explanation

What to accept:

  • The incidents happened
  • They're documented
  • They will come up in credentialing
  • You can't erase them
  • People will ask about them

Therapist's guidance: "You have to find a way to reconcile that so that you aren't anxious about it and you could, one, learn how to talk about it, so that you are open and honest and transparent with people."

Why this matters: Acceptance allows you to release anxiety about things you cannot change

2 Embrace Transparency - Proactively Tell Your Story

What this means: Don't wait for others to discover or interpret - YOU tell the story first

Include in your narrative:

  • What happened
  • Your mistakes (honest acknowledgment)
  • The full context (systemic issues, circumstances)
  • What you learned
  • How you've grown

Why proactive: Taking control of narrative prevents others from defining you solely by mistakes

The power of transparency: "The best thing about transparency if you do it right, is that you can take accountability and you can provide understanding."

3 Take Accountability for Your Part

What this means: Own what you did wrong, honestly and directly

BUT ALSO: Provide the full picture, including systemic failures

The distinction:

  • Your part: Specific actions or decisions you made
  • NOT your part: Systemic dysfunction, inadequate resources, being only doctor available

Example of balanced accountability:

  • "Yes, I made this specific mistake in judgment..."
  • "...AND I was the only physician available for this patient population..."
  • "...AND the system lacked adequate support structures..."
  • "...AND I went above and beyond in trying circumstances"

Why this matters: Accountability without context is accepting blame for systemic failures

4 Prepare Your Narrative in Writing

What this means: Write out complete story - for your own processing AND for future explanations

What to include:

  • What happened: Full chronology of events
  • Systemic issues: Context that contributed to incidents
  • Your actions: What you did, including mistakes
  • Your efforts: Ways you tried to address problems
  • Your reflections: What you learned, how you've grown

Two purposes:

  • Processing: Helps you understand and accept what happened
  • Preparation: Ready for future credentialing, interviews, explanations

Store safely: Keep this document for reference when needed

5 Use Transparency to Reduce Anxiety and Build Trust

What this means: Transparency as active tool, not just passive honesty

How transparency reduces anxiety:

  • No more hiding or fearing discovery
  • You control the narrative and timing
  • Proactive rather than reactive
  • Nothing left unsaid or unexplained

How transparency builds trust:

  • Shows honesty and self-awareness
  • Demonstrates accountability
  • Reveals your full humanity (not just mistakes)
  • Allows others to see context and growth

Therapist's wisdom: "Your story is powerful - telling it honestly can build empathy and trust."

Why This Framework Works

Acceptance allows you to release anxiety about things you cannot change.

Transparency builds credibility and allows others to see the full context, not just the negative mark.

Taking control of your narrative prevents others from defining you solely by your mistakes.

"If you want to allow this thing to define you, which it can, then so be it. But if you don't, then you have to understand that people are going to do their due diligence and you're gonna have to provide a reasonable explanation for whatever."

You're not defined by these incidents. You have agency in how you present yourself.

🛡️ Framework #2: Boundaries and Assertiveness

"Kind can be mean. I'll say that. But not nice. Because I think that a lot of times you choose nice over kind. And kind has boundaries."

The Five-Step Framework

1 Recognize Unfair Treatment

What this means: Notice when you're being treated unfairly or when someone is overstepping

Signs you're being treated unfairly:

  • Being blamed for systemic failures
  • Receiving harsher consequences than situation warrants
  • Having your narrative ignored or dismissed
  • Being disrespected or insulted
  • Facing double standards

With ex-husband specifically: Insults, false narratives, combative approach in mediation

2 Respond with Kindness, Not Just Niceness

Critical distinction:

NICENESS:

  • Avoiding conflict at your own expense
  • Being agreeable even when you disagree
  • Accepting mistreatment to keep peace
  • Prioritizing others' comfort over your own needs

KINDNESS:

  • Truth-telling, even when uncomfortable
  • Setting boundaries clearly
  • Protecting your well-being
  • Being honest about impact of others' behavior

Therapist's insight: "Kind is truth telling. Kind is I'm a show you yourself."

"Kind can be mean. I'll say that. But not nice."

3 Call Out Inappropriate Behavior

What this means: Name what's happening and refuse to engage with disrespect

What you've started doing with ex-husband:

  • Refusing to respond to insults
  • Calling out false narratives
  • Setting boundaries in mediation

Therapist's encouragement: "Amp it up" - be even more assertive

What "amping it up" looks like:

  • "That's disrespectful and I won't engage with it"
  • "That's factually incorrect. Here's what actually happened."
  • "You can't speak to me that way"
  • "I'm ending this conversation until you can be civil"

4 Return Negative Energy Appropriately

What this means: Don't absorb mistreatment - give it back

Therapist's framework: "A part of being mentally well is getting rid of the energy that people give you."

How to return negative energy:

  • Name the behavior and its impact
  • Set clear boundaries
  • Refuse to accept blame that isn't yours
  • Call out manipulation or disrespect
  • Document inappropriate communications

What NOT to do: Absorb it silently, internalize it, accept it as deserved

5 Practice in Both Professional and Personal Relationships

What this means: These skills apply everywhere

Professional settings:

  • Respond to unclear or negative communications in writing
  • Seek clarification when something is vague
  • Ensure your perspective is documented
  • Appeal factually incorrect reports
  • Gather evidence to support your case

Personal relationships:

  • Continue setting boundaries with ex-husband
  • Refuse to engage with insults or false narratives
  • Be "kind" (truthful, boundary-setting) not just "nice" (conflict-avoidant)

Why This Framework Works

Setting boundaries protects your mental health and prevents burnout.

Assertiveness ensures that others respect your needs and that you do not become a passive recipient of mistreatment.

"A part of being mentally well is getting rid of the energy that people give you."

You don't have to absorb others' negativity, disrespect, or unfair treatment.

Kindness includes boundaries. Being kind to yourself sometimes means being perceived as "not nice" by others.
