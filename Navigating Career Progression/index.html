🚀 Navigating Career Progression

When Leadership Deflects: Building Your Own Path Forward

📍 The Situation: Stalled Advancement and Managerial Deflection

"If I were to give her a Pokemon card, deflect would be one of her moves."

Where You Are

Current phase: Period of relative downtime at work (typically used for internal optimization and reflection)

Approaching milestone: Third year at the company

What You've Been Seeking

  • Career progression
  • A raise
  • Recognition matched with appropriate compensation

Your Contributions (Recognized but Not Rewarded)

  • Mentoring associates
  • Representing the company at conferences
  • Delivering value consistently

Yet: Your value is not being matched with appropriate recognition or compensation

The Pattern: Managerial Deflection

What keeps happening: Your manager consistently deflects or avoids substantive conversations about advancement

How deflection shows up:

  • Offering vague affirmations without concrete plans
  • No timelines provided
  • Changing the subject when you bring up promotion
  • Avoiding direct answers about raises

Your observation: "It's the whole carrot at the end of the stick sort of thing. But not even the carrot at this point. It's like sprinkling bread crumbs."

Your Boss's Motivations

What drives her: Highly motivated by financial outcomes

What she prioritizes: Immediate revenue over staff development

The reality: "Career progression sounds very nice... but that career progression piece also costs money."

The core problem: Persistent mismatch between your career aspirations and your manager's avoidance of meaningful engagement around promotion and compensation.

What this creates: You feel undervalued and uncertain, with no clear "progress bar" for advancement.

The brutal truth: "There is no progress bar to this. It's just me going, like, it's like a slot machine."

What you must do: Drive your own career progression rather than relying on managerial support.

🎯 Framework #1: The "Three Scenarios" Decision Framework

"I have to set a sort of — since there is no progress bar, I have to build one."

The Five-Step Process

1 Identify All Realistic Outcomes

Your three scenarios:

  1. Promotion: You get the advancement and compensation you deserve at current company
  2. Stagnation: Things stay the same — no meaningful progress
  3. Leaving: You find a new opportunity elsewhere

2 Assign Timelines and Markers for Each Scenario

Set a personal deadline: For example, summer (after three years at the company)

Your timeline:

  • By summer: You need to see meaningful progress toward promotion
  • If no progress: You're prepared to pursue opportunities elsewhere
  • If vague promises continue: That's your signal to move on

Why this matters: Setting a deadline gives you control over your career path instead of passively waiting

3 Gather Objective Data

What to collect:

  • Updated resume: All current duties and achievements
  • Market research: What are similar roles paying?
  • Salary benchmarks: What's your market worth?
  • Job descriptions: What are other companies offering for your level?

Why this matters: Objective data supports your case and clarifies your value

4 Monitor for Engagement or Avoidance from Leadership

Watch for: Is your boss truly considering your advancement, or just stringing you along?

Track markers: (See Framework #3 for detailed markers)

5 Decide Based on Evidence and Personal Values

Not based on: Hope, vague promises, wishful thinking

Based on: Concrete evidence, timelines met (or not), your personal worth

The key insight: "If the ceiling here is the basement of another job, then it's almost a no-brainer."

✓ Why This Framework Matters

Moves you from passive waiting → active decision-making

Clarifies your options

Sets deadlines so you're not stuck indefinitely

Regains agency and reduces anxiety about uncertainty

📊 Framework #2: The "Floor and Ceiling" Model for Negotiation

"You can't even ask for the ceiling if the floor isn't met."

The Four-Step Process

1 Define Your Minimum and Ideal Outcomes

Your FLOOR (minimum acceptable outcome):

  • What's the absolute minimum you need to stay?
  • Minimum salary increase?
  • Minimum title change?
  • Minimum responsibility shift?

Your CEILING (ideal outcome):

  • What would make you truly satisfied?
  • Ideal compensation?
  • Ideal title and responsibilities?
  • Ideal growth trajectory?

2 Use Objective Data to Support Your Case

Base your floor and ceiling on:

  • Your actual job duties (what you do vs. what you're paid for)
  • Market rates for similar positions
  • Industry standards for your level
  • Your documented contributions and achievements

Not based on: What you hope for or what feels fair in the abstract

3 Track Whether Conversations Meet, Exceed, or Fall Short

After discussions with your boss, assess:

  • Did the offer meet your floor? If not, it's not acceptable.
  • Did it approach your ceiling? Great, negotiate upward.
  • Was there even a concrete offer, or just vague promises?

Reality check: "If the ceiling here is the basement of another job, then it's almost a no-brainer."

4 Prepare to Walk Away If Floor Is Not Met

The boundary: If your minimum acceptable outcome isn't met, you must be willing to leave

Why this matters: Having a floor prevents you from settling for less than your worth

The power: Being prepared to walk away gives you negotiating strength

✓ Why This Model Matters

Grounds negotiations in reality, not hope

Prevents settling for less than your worth

Clarifies when it's time to move on

🔍 Framework #3: "Markers of Engagement" Checklist

"At some point in time, you'll have to be very clear with her on what you notice."

The Three-Step Process

1 Identify Signs of Genuine Engagement

What genuine engagement looks like:

  • Thoughtful responses: She takes time to seriously consider your request
  • Concrete plans: Specific timelines, salary numbers, title changes outlined
  • Follow-through: She does what she says she'll do
  • Proactive communication: She brings up your development without prompting
  • Investment: Time and resources allocated to your growth

2 Note Signs of Avoidance

What deflection/avoidance looks like:

  • Deflection: Avoiding direct answers, changing the subject
  • Vague affirmations: "I support your growth" without specifics
  • Lack of follow-up: Promises made, nothing happens
  • Breadcrumbing: Just enough encouragement to keep you there, no real carrot
  • Financial deflection: "The company can't afford it right now"

Your observation: "If I were to give her a Pokemon card, deflect would be one of her moves."

3 Use These Markers to Decide Your Next Move

If you see genuine engagement: Continue investing in the current company

If you see avoidance/deflection: Pivot to serious job searching

The key question to ask your boss:

"How do you balance the two — company growth and my career progression?"

Why this question matters: Forces her to articulate how (or if) she's actually supporting your advancement
