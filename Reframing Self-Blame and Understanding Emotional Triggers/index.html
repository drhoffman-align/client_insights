🔍 Framework #1: Contextualizing Emotional Reactions

"You didn't throw a knife at him. You were pissed. Something fell on you. For the 10,000,000,000th time."

The Five-Step Process

1 Identify the Trigger

Ask: What actually happened?

Was there a clear provocation or stressor?

Example from your session: Something fell on you "for the 10,000,000,000th time"

Context matters: This wasn't random anger — there was a clear, repeated provocation

2 Assess the Frequency and Intensity

Ask: Is this a rare outburst or a constant pattern?

Important distinctions:

  • Rare outburst: Getting angry after something falls on you for the millionth time = understandable
  • Constant pattern: Angry all day, every day, over everything = concerning

The therapist's observation: "Everybody has to control themselves. Everybody gets pissed off."

3 Compare to Baseline

Ask: Has this been happening more often, or is it a return to an old pattern?

In your case:

  • Are these episodes new?
  • Or have responsibilities simply increased?
  • Is this about you changing, or circumstances changing?

The metaphor used: "Living in this moment for a while and can't come out of it" — distinguishes temporary emotional states from persistent issues

4 Evaluate the Impact

Ask: Is it causing functional problems at work, home, or with relationships?

Key distinction:

  • Getting angry but still functioning = normal emotional response
  • Getting angry and can't work, can't parent, relationships falling apart = needs intervention

"It is the actions that — sort of the actions are not being able to control reactions, not be able to control emotions or whatever — that I'm truly looking for."

5 Reframe the Narrative

Ask: Is this truly a personal failing, or a normal human reaction?

The therapist's validation: "Some of the things that you're being blamed for here, I do not — it doesn't sound like it's a you thing."

Reframe from: "I'm the worst person in the world" (catastrophizing)

Reframe to: "I got frustrated after something happened repeatedly, and that's understandable"

✓ Why This Framework Matters

Separates situational anger from pathological patterns

Reduces unnecessary self-blame

Clarifies when intervention is needed vs. when you're just human

💚 Framework #2: Self-Compassion and Reality Testing

"The way it comes to me sometimes is like it is absolutely you. But then when I ask you, like, what the heck is happening, there's a lot of reason in that."

The Four-Step Process

1 Notice Self-Critical Thoughts

Your pattern: "I'm the worst person in the world"

Other versions of this:

  • "I'm the problem"
  • "Something's wrong with me"
  • "I'm fiery and I live up to that"
  • "I can't control myself"

The therapist's observation: "You can sometimes believe that you are fiery and you live up to that, and sometimes you are."

2 Gather Evidence

Ask: What actually happened? How did others react?

Example from session:

  • What happened: Something fell on you for the millionth time
  • Your reaction: You got pissed and yelled
  • What you DIDN'T do: Throw a knife, become violent, lose complete control
  • Others' reaction: Suggested you take your medicine

The therapist's validation: "You didn't throw a knife at him. You were pissed."

3 Consider Alternative Explanations

Ask: Could this be a normal response to stress?

Alternative explanations:

  • You've been overwhelmed with increased responsibilities
  • You've been stuck indoors due to weather
  • Something annoying happened repeatedly
  • You're human and humans get frustrated

"It's not about you. Maybe it's not you."

"Some of the things that you're being blamed for here, I do not — it doesn't sound like it's a you thing."

4 Practice Self-Kindness

Remind yourself: Everyone has moments of frustration

Self-compassionate responses:

  • "I'm doing my best with increased stress"
  • "Getting frustrated doesn't make me the worst person"
  • "Everyone gets pissed off sometimes"
  • "My reaction was understandable given the circumstances"

The therapist's reminder: "Everybody has to control themselves. Everybody gets pissed off."
