⚡ Framework #1: Active vs. Passive Decision-Making

"Your decisions for your life are going to be long term. Never lasting."

The Four-Step Process

1 Recognize When You Are Making Decisions Passively

What this means: Notice when you're deciding out of habit, fear, or external pressure

Signs of passive decision-making:

  • Autopilot: "This is just what I do" without questioning
  • Fear-driven: "What will happen if I don't?"
  • External pressure: "They expect me to..."
  • Default mode: Continuing without reconsidering
  • Lack of reflection: Not thinking about what YOU want

Why this matters: You can't change what you don't notice

2 Identify What Truly Matters to You in Each Situation

What this means: Pause and ask yourself what's actually important here

Questions to ask:

  • What do I value most in this situation?
  • What would align with my long-term goals?
  • What would serve my well-being?
  • If I weren't afraid, what would I choose?
  • What would my future self thank me for?

Example from your life:

Decision: Whether to continue in demanding teaching role

Passive approach: "I have to because I need the paycheck and they're counting on me"

What truly matters: My well-being, sustainable work-life balance, long-term career goals, quality of life

3 Make Decisions Intentionally, with Clear Purpose and Desired Outcome

What this means: Choose with awareness, not on autopilot

How to make intentional decisions:

  • Pause before deciding: Don't react immediately
  • Clarify your purpose: Why am I making this choice?
  • Envision the outcome: What result do I want?
  • Consider alignment: Does this match my values?
  • Choose actively: "I am deciding to..." (not "I have to...")

The therapist's guidance: "It should feel intentional. It should feel like you're driven for it and by it."

Example:

Passive: "I have to go to the gym because I always do"

Intentional: "I'm choosing to go to the gym today because I want to build strength and it energizes me" OR "I'm choosing to rest today because my body needs recovery"

4 Reflect on the Results and Adjust as Needed

What this means: Notice how your intentional decisions feel and what they create

Questions to reflect on:

  • How did that decision work out?
  • Do I feel more aligned with my values?
  • What would I do differently next time?
  • Am I building the life I want?

Why this matters: Intentional choices create a sense of agency and alignment with personal values

✓ Benefits of Active Decision-Making

Sense of control: You're shaping your life, not just reacting to it

Alignment: Your choices match your values and goals

Satisfaction: Living intentionally creates fulfillment

Progress: Like having a plan at the gym, you move toward what matters

Energy: Less wasted effort on things that don't serve you

🌿 Framework #2: Prioritizing Self-Care and Rest

"Prioritize your life. Not your work because work is gonna get work."

The Three-Step Process

1 Schedule Time for Self-Reflection and Rest

What this means: Make it a non-negotiable part of your day, especially mornings before work

The therapist's guidance: "Find time to just think about you. And write."

Specific recommendation:

  • 30 minutes daily for self-reflection
  • Preferably in the morning before work starts
  • Use pencil and paper to clarify thoughts and decisions
  • Focus on YOU — your needs, goals, feelings

Why this matters: Without time to reflect, you stay in reactive mode

2 Recognize the Difference Between Doing Things for Work and Doing Things for Yourself

What this means: Your medication, energy, and time should support YOUR LIFE, not just your job

The critical insight: "You don't need meds for work. You need meds for life."

How this applies:

  • Medication timing: Should support your life outside of work, not just job performance
  • Energy allocation: Save some for yourself, not just work tasks
  • Decision-making: "Will this serve MY life or just my job?"
  • Priorities: Your well-being comes before work demands

The therapist's reminder: "Prioritize your life. Not your work because work is gonna get work."

What this means: Work will always get done because of external motivators (paycheck, expectations), but your life needs YOUR OWN motivation and attention

3 Allow Yourself Rest Days and Avoid Overworking

What this means: Rest is not lazy — it's essential and productive

Permission to rest:

  • Skip the gym when your body needs recovery
  • Take mental health days when overwhelmed
  • Say no to extra obligations when depleted
  • Recognize rest as productive — it restores your capacity

Why this matters: Rest and self-care are essential for long-term well-being. Overworking and neglecting rest is a sign of survival mode, which is unsustainable.

Reframe:

Old belief: "Resting is lazy. I should push through."

New understanding: "Rest restores me so I can show up fully. Pushing through exhaustion is survival mode, not strength."

✓ Why Self-Care and Rest Matter

Sustainability: You can't maintain high performance without recovery

Clarity: Reflection time helps you make better decisions

Well-being: Your life quality depends on prioritizing yourself

Intentionality: Morning reflection sets the tone for purposeful days

Balance: Life is more than work — you need energy for all of it
