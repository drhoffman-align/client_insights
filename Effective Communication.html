How to Communicate
with Someone You Care About

A guide for navigating understanding, conflict, and connection

The Foundation

Communication is the only tool humans have to understand one another. Without it, you are not connecting — you are assuming. And assumptions, left unchecked, become the source of most of our pain in relationships.

01

Understanding Requires More Than Good Intentions

We understand people through two things: knowledge and experience. Knowledge is what we know about someone — their history, their values, how they think. Experience is what we've actually lived alongside them — the moments that showed us who they are beyond words.

Neither one is enough on its own. And neither happens without communication.

People don't know anything unless you tell them. Even if you think they should know — it is always safer and clearer to communicate it directly.

Not communicating leaves too much to chance. When you speak, you know they've heard you. Then their actions can tell you whether they care. Silence tells you nothing — it only leaves you guessing.

02

Words Are Our Best Tool — Use Them Carefully

Words are how we communicate most precisely. Even so, two people can speak the same language and still misunderstand each other — because culture, upbringing, beliefs, and personal history all shape what words mean to each of us.

Clarity in meaning comes from shared understanding. And the only way to know what something means to someone else is to ask — not assume.

```
Verbal Communication
  • GoodImmediate connection; tone and presence add emotional context
  • GoodReal-time clarification — misunderstandings can be caught quickly
  • GoodWhen regulated, it deepens connection fast
  • RiskTriggers defensiveness quickly — the brain detects threat and shuts down understanding
  • RiskYou respond to how something feels, not what's being said
  • RiskOnce it's out, you can't revise it
Written Communication
  • GoodSlows the process down — keeps your thinking brain online
  • GoodYou can organize thoughts instead of just reacting
  • GoodUnlimited attempts to say it right instead of fast
  • GoodReceiver can process without social pressure
  • RiskNo vocal tone — meaning can be distorted
  • RiskCan become avoidance if used to never engage directly
```
—

The Real Issue Isn't Verbal vs. Written

Most people don't have a communication problem. They have a regulation problem disguised as a communication problem. The method matters less than the state you're in when you use it.

```
Unregulated + Verbal

An argument. You're reacting, not communicating.

Unregulated + Written

A passive-aggressive essay. Still an attack, just slower.

Regulated + Verbal

Connection. Real-time understanding and repair.

Regulated + Written

Clarity. Intentional, structured, boundary-setting.

Writing isn't avoidance. Done well, writing is regulation — it creates the distance your nervous system needs to communicate instead of react.

```
—

How to Prepare — Before the Conversation Starts

Regardless of whether you choose to speak or write, preparation changes everything. Start by knowing your own pattern.

```
Know Your Pattern First

Do you tend to avoid conflict and then explode? Over-explain and still feel unheard? Shut down the moment you feel challenged? If you don't know your pattern, you'll misuse both methods.

Even if you plan to talk in person, write first. Use writing to clarify what you actually feel — separate facts from interpretations, and get clear on your goal before you open your mouth.

Simple Writing Structure
1.
What happened — just the facts, no interpretation
2.
How it affected me — what I felt and why
3.
What I need going forward — be specific
Use Writing First When
  • →Emotions are running high
  • →You tend to get reactive or shut down
  • →You need to set a clear boundary
  • →There's a history of conflict in this relationship
Use Verbal When
  • →Both people can stay regulated
  • →There's mutual respect in the room
  • →You're building connection, not just correcting
  • →Nuance and back-and-forth matter

The sweet spot: write first for clarity, then follow up verbally for connection. This combination reduces miscommunication, emotional escalation, and "I didn't know" excuses.

```
03

Before You Talk — Know Your Purpose

Before any difficult conversation, ask yourself what you actually want from it. Your answer will shape everything about how you show up.

```
Ask
Do you love this person?
If yes, let that guide your tone — even when it's hard.
Ask
Do you want to move past this and continue the relationship?
Then resolution matters more than being right.
Ask
Are you considering ending the relationship?
That is a valid purpose too — and communicating it clearly is still an act of respect.

You can love someone and still do what is best for you — even if it hurts them. They will hurt now or later. Later usually means more time lost and a deeper wound.

```
04

During the Conversation — Stay Out of Argument Mode

Arguing and communicating are not the same thing. When you are arguing, your defenses are up. When your defenses are up, you are focused on protecting yourself — not on understanding the other person or being understood.

```
Do
Listen to understand, not to respond
When the other person is speaking, think about what they are saying and how they arrived at those thoughts. Then say back what you understand before anything else.
Do
Validate before you correct
Even if your intentions were misread, acknowledge how they landed first. "I understand how that came across — that wasn't my intention, and here's what I meant."
Do
Name the purpose when things escalate
If the conversation starts to turn into an argument, say it out loud: "I want to remind us both — the reason we're talking is because I care about this and I want to work through it with you." This can lower both of your defenses.
If you keep arguing

Stop the conversation. Nothing productive will come from continuing, and it will likely leave both of you more hurt.

Walking away is not defeat — it is protecting the relationship from what an escalated argument will do to it. Come back when both of you are regulated.

Before the next attempt: slow your body down first. Breathing matters more than people think. Set a goal of being understood — not winning.

```
05

Resolve It — Together

Resolution doesn't mean one person wins. It means both people feel heard and both people have a shared sense of how to move forward.

End the conversation by restating the purpose you identified at the start, and collectively deciding what comes next. Not what you want. What you both want.

Clarity is the whole point of communication. Avoidance never brings clarity — it only delays and deepens the problem.

Reflection — Before Your Next Conversation

What am I afraid will happen if I say exactly what I mean?

What am I assuming the other person already knows — that I've never actually told them?

What is my purpose in this conversation, and am I willing to hold onto it even when it gets uncomfortable?

Am I trying to be understood — or am I trying to win?

Am I regulated enough right now to communicate — or am I reacting?
