🧭 Reclaiming Agency Amidst Change

Building Stability After Loss

The Situation

You are navigating several major stressors hitting simultaneously — not one at a time, the way you'd plan for them, but all at once. Job loss. Housing instability. Family grief. A worsening situation with a parent. A relationship on pause until conditions stabilize. This is not a manageable pile. It's a cascade.

Work Contract ending within a month. Decision pending: leave early for a payout or stay through August.
Housing Must find new housing for yourself and your pets with limited support and a compressed timeline.
Family Recent loss of a close family member. A parent's dementia worsening inside a complicated family system.
Connection Isolated from your usual community. Your relationship is on hold. The support structures you relied on are gone.

Underneath all of it: a sense of being in shock. Low energy. Difficulty getting out of bed. A grief not just for what you've lost, but for who you were inside those structures.

Core Problem

The stressors are real — but the deeper issue is what they've done to your relationship with your own agency. You've shifted into reactive mode: getting by, filling in whatever blanks life hands you, managing one crisis at a time without directing your own path.

What makes this particularly sharp is the mismatch. The evidence of your past capability is substantial. You have navigated hard things. You have created change when it mattered. You've shown up fully — for jobs, for communities, for other people. The question this session raises is: why is it so hard to do it for yourself?

"You were all in when it wasn't for you. Now that it's for you, you're not."

Understanding the Dynamics

Ego Death

The painful process of losing old identities, roles, and beliefs about yourself — often after major life upheaval. It's disorienting because the self you relied on is gone before the new one has taken shape. It's also, eventually, an opening.

Agency

The ability to make choices and influence your own outcomes. You have it. The current crisis has made it feel inaccessible — but your history shows it's there. The work is reconnecting with it.

Rumination

Getting stuck in repetitive, circular, negative thinking. Worries loop without resolution when they stay in your head. Externalizing them — getting them onto paper — interrupts the cycle.

Reactive vs. Strategic Mode

Reactive mode responds to whatever appears next. Strategic mode plans, sequences, and makes decisions ahead of the crisis. You are capable of both — right now you're stuck in the first one.

🎬 Framework 1: The Deputy Director Model

Moving from managing your life to leading it

You described yourself as the "deputy director" of your own life — technically in charge, but not quite claiming the role. The director's chair is yours. The question is whether you're willing to sit in it.

Deputy Director

Responding to what arrives. Managing crises. Filling in the blanks of whatever change brings. Letting external events set the agenda.

→
Director

Setting the agenda. Making decisions ahead of the crisis. Planning with intention. Treating your life like a project that deserves strategy.

1

Recognize Who's Currently Running Things

Notice the moments when "change" or circumstances are making your decisions for you. Name it when it's happening. You can't redirect something you haven't identified.

2

Recall the Evidence

You have found jobs before. Built community from scratch. Made significant things happen when they mattered. The capability that created those outcomes still belongs to you. It didn't leave with the circumstances that are gone.

3

Strategize Before You React

Make lists. Block out time. Sequence your next moves. Treat housing and employment as projects with steps, not walls to run into. The strategist in you knows how to do this.

4

Claim the Role

Accept that you have power over more than you currently feel you do. Not everything — but more. The difference between deputy and director is not capability. It's willingness to accept responsibility for the outcome.

The Ego Death Reframe

The identity loss you're experiencing is real and painful. But ego death is also the precondition for something new. The roles you've lost were not always yours to carry — some of them needed to go. What's on the other side of this isn't just recovery. It's an opportunity to build something more authentically yours.

"Spend less time in your head than you do on paper."

📓 Framework 2: Truth-Telling Journaling

Getting worries out of your head and onto paper — where they can be solved
1

Write Daily — Ideally Outside Your Home

A coffee shop, a park, anywhere that isn't the space where you've been stuck. The change of environment matters. It signals to your nervous system that something is different today.

2

Externalize Every Worry

Write down every fear, every "what if," every negative loop you've been running. Don't filter. The goal is to empty the mental queue onto paper, where each worry can be seen for what it actually is — rather than what it feels like at 3am.

3

Problem-Solve on Paper

Once a worry is written, respond to it directly. What is actually true? What is catastrophizing? What is solvable? What is outside your control? The page is where rumination becomes analysis.

4

Capture Throughout the Day

When a worry surfaces between sessions, write it down immediately — phone notes, scraps of paper, anything. Bring them to your next journaling block to process. The goal is that nothing stays trapped in your head unexamined.

Why This Works for You Specifically

You are someone who processes internally, which means worries compound invisibly. Writing forces the loop to complete — and gives you something concrete to argue with, adjust, or discard. The journal is not a diary. It's a problem-solving tool.

The Logic of No Negotiation

The brain at waking is not your most rational brain. It will find reasons to delay, to wait, to see how you feel first. Don't ask it. Place the medication where there's no decision to make. The action precedes the motivation — not the other way around.

Putting It Into Practice

  • Review your résumé — not as a job tool, but as evidence. Look at what you built, where you were, what you created. The person who did those things is still you. Read it as a reminder of capability, not a checklist of the past.

  • Treat housing and employment as projects, not emergencies. Make lists. Identify concrete next steps. Block time for searching. Sequenced action is less overwhelming than a mass of unresolved pressure.

  • Let yourself grieve without making it mean something is broken. The losses are real. Grieving them is not the same as being stuck. The ego death you're in the middle of is a transition — painful, not permanent.

  • Be cautious with family systems during this window. Worsening dementia in a parent, complicated by a toxic family dynamic, is a high-drain environment. Protect your emotional resources. Know the difference between showing up and being consumed.
