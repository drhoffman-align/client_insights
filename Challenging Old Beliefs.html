Therapeutic Insights

Alignment Psychiatry

The Situation

You're navigating a complex health situation with elevated blood pressure (140-150/100) while managing ADHD. You've stopped taking Adderall due to cardiovascular concerns and are awaiting cardiology referral and lab results. Meanwhile, you're experiencing migraines, panic attacks, and significant stress as your father's primary caregiver.

"Your strict religious upbringing created beliefs equating self-sacrifice with moral goodness, leading to chronic neglect of both your physical and emotional needs."

This pattern didn't develop overnight—it's rooted in deeply held beliefs from childhood that continue to shape how you care for yourself today.

Core Problem

The central challenge isn't just about blood pressure or ADHD medication—it's about the deeply rooted core beliefs from childhood that drive you to prioritize others at your own expense. You've internalized the idea that self-care is selfish, which results in neglecting medical care, inconsistent medication use, and unsustainable self-sacrifice.

This pattern ultimately harms your health and well-being, making it impossible to sustainably care for those you love.

Clinical Concepts

Core Beliefs
These are deeply held assumptions about yourself, others, and the world that formed in childhood. They may be outdated or unverified, yet they continue to shape your automatic responses and decisions.
Schema
A mental framework that drives automatic patterns in your life. Your current schema operates on the principle: "I must put others first to be worthy." This belief system runs in the background, influencing countless daily decisions.
Catastrophizing
The tendency to expect worst-case scenarios, particularly around disappointing others. This thinking pattern keeps you trapped in self-neglect, as you imagine dire consequences for prioritizing your own needs.

Understanding these concepts helps you see that your struggle isn't a personal failing—it's the result of learned patterns that can be examined, questioned, and ultimately changed.

Therapeutic Framework

We're using Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) to understand and change the chain of events that leads from your core beliefs to your actions. This framework shows how your thoughts, emotions, and behaviors are all connected:

1
Core Beliefs shape your worldview and create the foundation for how you interpret experiences
2
Thoughts arise automatically from these beliefs when you encounter situations
3
Emotions follow naturally from your thoughts, creating feelings that seem inevitable
4
Actions stem from your emotions, completing the chain from belief to behavior
"By mapping this chain from beliefs to actions, you can identify where to intervene and create meaningful change in your patterns of self-neglect."

Practical Instructions

Medication Plan

Week 1: Start Adderall 7.5mg daily. Take your blood pressure before and after the medication each day. Take it consistently with morning food.

Week 2: If your blood pressure remains stable, increase to 15mg daily. Continue monitoring before and after.

⚠ Safety Warnings

Stop medication immediately if blood pressure exceeds 150/100. Also monitor for headaches, anxiety, or heart palpitations. Remember: inconsistent use causes more instability than not taking medication at all.

Clinical Rationale

This stepwise approach balances your need for ADHD symptom management with cardiovascular safety through careful monitoring. We're prioritizing your well-being while addressing your symptoms.

Homework

Complete these assignments over the next month before your follow-up session:

  • Research the CBT theory materials provided to deepen your understanding of how beliefs shape behavior
  • Monitor your blood pressure daily and maintain a medication log with times and readings
  • Journal about situations where your old beliefs drive self-neglect—notice the pattern in action
  • Reflect on whose rules you're following and whether they serve you now as an adult
  • Notice small opportunities to prioritize your needs without catastrophizing the consequences

This homework isn't about perfection—it's about awareness. Simply noticing these patterns is the first step toward changing them.

Key Takeaways

  • Self-care is necessary, not selfish—you cannot pour from an empty cup
  • Consistent medication use is crucial for both safety and effectiveness
  • Core beliefs from your upbringing drive current self-neglect patterns
  • Daily blood pressure monitoring is essential when restarting medication
  • Inherited rules can be questioned and changed—they aren't absolute truths
  • Small acts of self-prioritization enable sustainable well-being for everyone
  • CBT maps the chain from beliefs to actions, showing you where to intervene
  • Deconstructing old beliefs is gradual but necessary work
  • You can honor your parents' intentions without replicating harmful patterns
  • Reconstructing your morals to be "you-centered" is the therapeutic goal

Bottom Line

The beliefs you learned in childhood—that self-sacrifice equals goodness and self-care equals selfishness—are keeping you trapped in patterns that harm your health and limit your ability to care for others sustainably. Through CBT, you're learning to identify and challenge these outdated beliefs, replacing them with a more balanced understanding: taking care of yourself isn't just acceptable, it's essential. Your worth isn't determined by how much you sacrifice, and prioritizing your health doesn't make you selfish—it makes you wise. This journey of reconstructing your core beliefs takes time and compassion, but it's the path toward genuine, sustainable well-being for both you and those you love.
