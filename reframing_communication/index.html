📍 The Recurring Pattern: When Respect Feels Uncertain

"We have to change things if we want to get aligned in the marriage."

The Core Dynamic Identified in Previous Sessions

When respect, alignment, or follow-through feel uncertain:

Your nervous system responds by seeking to restore stability through direct clarification, correction, or urgency

Why this makes sense: You have strong values around structure, reliability, and visible partnership alignment

Your core belief: Actions and follow-through demonstrate care and respect

⚠️ The Escalation Cycle

Current pattern:

  1. Trigger: Something feels disrespectful or misaligned
  2. Your response: Direct correction or urgent clarification
  3. Kate's experience: Interaction feels like criticism or emotional pressure
  4. Kate's response: Withdrawal or defensiveness
  5. Your interpretation: Withdrawal confirms that alignment/respect is not present
  6. Cycle restarts: Reinforced sense of misalignment

The Underlying Issues from Your Relationship Report

When communication becomes more corrective or urgent:

  • Kate may experience it as criticism or emotional pressure
  • This can lead to withdrawal or defensiveness
  • Her withdrawal reinforces your sense that respect/alignment is absent
  • This restarts the cycle
The therapeutic goal: Not to revisit the conceptual framework again, but to begin practicing ways to interrupt this cycle in real time.

The shift: From conceptual understanding → to behavioral experimentation and collaborative communication tools.

🎯 Intent vs. Impact: Why It Matters

"When someone does something intentionally to harm you… it makes a bigger difference."

The Framework

Distinguishing Intentional from Unintentional Harm

The therapist's question: "Why does it matter if it's intentional or unintentional?"

Two types of harm:

  • Intentional harm: Someone deliberately tries to hurt, undermine, or disrespect you
  • Unintentional harm: Someone causes hurt without meaning to

Why this distinction matters:

  • Intentional harm requires different response (boundaries, consequences)
  • Unintentional harm can be repaired through communication and understanding
  • Assuming malicious intent when none exists escalates conflict unnecessarily

Reframing Your Interpretation

Current interpretation: "She's trying to make me look like a liar" / "She's undermining me"

This assumes: Malicious intent

Alternative interpretation: Maybe she wasn't trying to undermine you — maybe she just didn't realize how it would land

This allows: Space for dialogue rather than immediate defense

The therapist's reframe attempt: Shift from "She undermined me" toward "Maybe it wasn't intentional"

The Intensity Perception Mismatch

The therapist's key insight: "Your level five may feel like her level nine."

What this means:

  • Different people experience the same emotional intensity differently
  • What feels like moderate frustration to you may feel like high threat to Kate
  • Your nervous system and her nervous system have different reactivity thresholds

The result: "Her reaction may come from consistency of not knowing what to do" — she may enter a freeze response

The freeze response: When Kate shuts down, it's not necessarily resistance or disrespect — it may be a defensive regulation response when she perceives threat.

Fight / Flight / Freeze / Fawn: Her "freeze" is a neurobiological response to feeling overwhelmed, not a character flaw.

🔧 The Harm Repair Model

"Apology protects you… But that's not all involved in harm reduction."

Moving Beyond "I Apologized"

Why Apology Alone Isn't Enough

The therapist's observation: "Apology protects you… But that's not all involved in harm reduction."

Your perspective: "I apologized" should be sufficient — the action has been acknowledged

Kate's experience: The apology doesn't address how it impacted her emotionally

The Full Harm Repair Model

Steps for relational repair:

  1. Accountability: Acknowledge what you did
  2. Understanding impact: "How did this affect you?"
  3. Emotional validation: "I can see why that hurt"
  4. Behavioral change: "What can I do differently?"

The shift the therapist encourages:

From: "I apologized" (accountability model)

To: "How did this impact you?" (emotional understanding model)

The therapist's guidance: "Ask how it impacted her."

Prioritizing Her Emotional Experience

The therapist's framing: "In a way it's prioritizing her emotions."

What this means:

  • Relational repair sometimes requires temporarily centering partner's emotional experience
  • This doesn't invalidate YOUR emotional experience
  • It creates space for her to feel heard before problem-solving

This framework resembles Emotionally Focused Therapy (EFT) principles

❓ The Curiosity-Based Communication Strategy

"Approach things like Kate is not trying to harm you."

The Two Behavioral Adjustments

1 Temporarily Assume Non-Malicious Intent

What this means: When something feels disrespectful or misaligned, pause and assume Kate isn't trying to harm you

This does NOT mean:

  • Invalidating your emotional experience
  • Accepting disrespectful behavior
  • Giving up on accountability

It DOES mean:

  • Preventing your nervous system from escalating before gathering more information
  • Shifting from hostile attribution → benevolent attribution
  • Creating space for dialogue instead of immediate defense

The therapist's guidance: "Approach things like Kate is not trying to harm you."

2 Use Curiosity-Based Questions Before Correction

Instead of: Immediate correction or urgent clarification

Try asking: "Help me understand what happened here"

Other curiosity-based questions:

  • "Did this open something else?"
  • "Is there another issue here?"
  • "What were you thinking when that happened?"
  • "Can you help me understand your perspective?"

The goal: Move from defensive conversation → exploratory conversation

The technique: Use questions instead of conclusions

The therapist's guidance: "Maybe ask… did this open something else? Is there another issue here?"

✓ Why This Creates Space for Repair

Questions slow down the escalation sequence

Creates dialogue rather than monologue

Allows Kate to explain rather than defend

Increases likelihood of collaborative repair

🔄 Shifting the Interaction Pattern

"Try asking questions. Just try it. Let me know what happens."

⚠️ Old Pattern

Trigger → Correction → Withdrawal → Reinforced misalignment

✓ New Pattern (Goal)

Trigger → Curiosity → Clarification → Validation → Collaborative repair

The Behavioral Experiment

This is classic CBT technique: Test a new communication strategy and observe the outcome

Steps for the experiment:

  1. Assume no malicious intent when something feels off
  2. Ask questions instead of correcting
  3. Observe what happens — does Kate respond differently?
  4. Report back in next session

The therapist's homework: "Give me a couple incidences where you approached it with questions."

Important Clarifications

This strategy is NOT about:

  • Dismissing accountability
  • Minimizing your needs
  • Permanently changing your communication style immediately
  • Giving up on respect and alignment mattering

This strategy IS about:

  • Slowing the escalation sequence
  • Reducing withdrawal and increasing likelihood of repair
  • Allowing underlying concerns (respect, prioritization, intimacy, alignment) to be discussed more productively
  • Gathering information about how different approaches affect the interaction cycle

📚 Understanding Emotional Awareness Differences

Alexithymia: Difficulty Identifying Emotions

The therapist's insight: "Most people don't know how they feel. People are alexithymic."

What alexithymia means: Difficulty identifying or describing emotions

Clinical purpose: Normalize Kate's emotional confusion — it's not resistance or avoidance, it's genuinely not knowing

Cultural and Gender Emotional Socialization

The therapist's observation: "Black men often learn not to consider emotions."

Emotional expression influenced by:

  • Gender socialization
  • Cultural norms
  • Survival strategies

What this means: Different people have different access to emotional awareness based on how they were raised

The Johari Window: Understanding Blind Spots

Four areas of self-awareness:

  1. Known to self, known to others: What you both see clearly
  2. Unknown to self, known to others: Your blind spots
  3. Known to self, unknown to others: What you haven't shared
  4. Unknown to self, unknown to others: Unconscious processes

Purpose: Increase humility about self-knowledge — everyone has blind spots
