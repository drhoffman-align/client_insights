🎯 The Core Problem: Stuck in Survival Mode

"Survival mode is like being in a bubble — you can't see or care about anything outside of it."

What's Really Happening

The main issue: You're experiencing heightened anxiety and emotional volatility that's interfering with your ability to:

  • Empathize with your partner
  • Connect meaningfully in relationships
  • Feel grounded in your identity
  • Access calm, broader perspective
  • Think long-term or strategically

The Mismatch

Your internal experience: Sense of urgency, danger, constant vigilance

The actual reality: While challenges exist, you're not in imminent danger

The gap: Your nervous system is responding as if threats are immediate and severe, even when they're not

The result: You're stuck in "survival mode" — preoccupied with immediate stressors and unable to access the part of your brain that can plan, reflect, and connect

What Survival Mode Looks Like

Signs you're experiencing it:

  • Narrowed focus: Can only see immediate problems
  • Increased reactivity: Quick to irritation or defensiveness
  • Difficulty empathizing: Hard to care about others' needs unless they directly impact you
  • Preoccupation: Mind constantly churning over problems
  • Disconnection: Feeling distant from partner and relationships
  • Identity confusion: "Who am I?" feels impossible to answer
This is not a character flaw. "It's not that you don't care — it's that you're stuck in survival mode."

This is your brain doing what it's designed to do when it perceives threat — prioritize immediate safety over everything else.

The challenge: Your brain is responding to perceived threats (unemployment, relationship stress, identity questions) as if they were immediate physical dangers, even though they're not.

🛡️ Framework #1: Survival Mode vs. Executive Functioning

"Practice telling yourself you're okay. Right now, you're not in imminent danger."

The Four-Step Process

1 Recognize When You're in Survival Mode

What survival mode is: Brain state that prioritizes immediate safety, triggering fight/flight/freeze/fawn responses

The brain science: "Your brain makes a habit out of what you do with it." "You have a lot more practice with your emotions than you do with your prefrontal cortex."

The metaphor: "Your brain is like a car with two engines: the emotional engine (old, powerful) and the thinking engine (newer, still developing)."

Why this matters: Survival mode narrows focus, increases reactivity, and makes it hard to empathize or think long-term

2 Notice the Signs

How to tell you're in survival mode:

  • Irritability: Snapping at partner, short fuse
  • Preoccupation: Can't stop thinking about problems
  • Difficulty caring about others' needs: Unless they directly impact you
  • Feeling stuck in a bubble: Can't see outside immediate concerns
  • Physical tension: Tight shoulders, clenched jaw, shallow breathing
  • Mental fog: Hard to make decisions or think clearly

The insight: "Emotions are energy modulators; anxiety and anger are like twin sisters driving your reactions."

Why this matters: Awareness is the first step to shifting out of this state

3 Practice Self-Reassurance

What to do: Tell yourself, "I am okay right now. I am not in imminent danger."

When to use it:

  • When you notice irritability rising
  • Before reacting in conversations with partner
  • When anxiety feels overwhelming
  • Multiple times throughout the day

How to do it:

  • Pause what you're doing
  • Take 2-3 deep breaths
  • Say it out loud or in your mind: "I am okay right now"
  • Look around and notice: What's actually happening? Am I safe?
  • Repeat as needed until you feel calmer

Why this works: This calms the nervous system and allows the executive (thinking) part of the brain to come online

4 Slow Down and Give Yourself Time to Process

What this means: Don't rush to react — pause, breathe, think

Practical application:

  • In conversations with partner: "Can I think about that and get back to you?"
  • When feeling irritable: Step away for a few minutes before responding
  • Before making decisions: Sleep on it, don't rush
  • During anxiety spikes: Sit with it, let it pass, don't act on impulse

Why this matters: Time and self-reassurance help shift from emotional reactivity to logical, values-based action

The encouragement: "Give yourself a little more grace. Your brain is still developing."

✓ What This Framework Gives You

Awareness: You can recognize when survival mode is running the show

Control: Tools to calm your nervous system and access thinking brain

Connection: Ability to empathize and engage with partner once you're out of survival mode

Perspective: Space to see that you're okay right now, even while challenges exist

🔍 Framework #2: Identity Clarification Process

"Identity is something that shifts because it's influenced by the shit you go through."

The Four-Step Process

1 List Characteristics About Yourself

What to do: Write down characteristics about yourself — good, bad, and neutral

Examples to include:

  • Traits: "I'm analytical," "I care about social justice," "I can be impatient"
  • Values: "Integrity matters to me," "I value learning"
  • Roles: "I'm a partner," "I'm a student," "I'm a job seeker"
  • Strengths: "I'm resilient," "I'm curious"
  • Challenges: "I struggle with anxiety," "I get irritable under stress"
  • Interests: "I care about education policy," "I enjoy..."

Important: Include ALL of it — not just positive or negative, but the full picture

2 Gather Feedback From Trusted People

What to do: Note what trusted people say about how they see you

Who to ask/observe:

  • Your partner
  • Close friends
  • Family members
  • Professors or mentors
  • Former colleagues or supervisors

What to listen for:

  • How they describe you to others
  • What they appreciate about you
  • What they find challenging
  • Patterns in feedback across multiple people

Why this matters: Others often see things about us that we can't see ourselves

3 Reflect on How Your Values and Identity Have Changed Over Time

What to consider:

  • Who were you 5 years ago? What mattered to you then?
  • What experiences have shaped you? (trauma, education, relationships, work)
  • What values have shifted? Why?
  • What parts of you have stayed constant?
  • How has the overdose eight weeks ago affected how you see yourself?

The key insight: Change is normal and expected

4 Accept That Identity Is Fluid and Influenced by Life Experiences

What this means: You're not supposed to have yourself "figured out" — identity evolves

The truth: "Identity is something that shifts because it's influenced by the shit you go through."

What to release:

  • The idea that identity should be fixed
  • Pressure to be "farther along"
  • Shame about not knowing who you are
  • Anxiety about changing values

What to embrace:

  • "You're learning. That's the whole point of all of this — learn and adapt."
  • Growth is messy
  • You're allowed to evolve
  • Uncertainty is part of the process

Why this matters: This process helps ground you in self-knowledge and reduces anxiety about change

The Three-Step Process

  • Morning medications
  • Getting sunlight exposure
  • Eating breakfast
  • Work/job search time
  • Gym or exercise

What to focus on during this time:

  • Reflecting on values and goals
  • Journaling about experiences
  • Practicing self-reassurance ("I am okay right now")
  • Planning your day with intention

Why this works: Gives your thinking brain time to come online before diving into tasks

What to do instead:

Why this matters: Social engagement (even passive) is important for mental health
