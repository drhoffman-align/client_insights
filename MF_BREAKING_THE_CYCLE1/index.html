🛡️ Framework #1: Recognizing and Disengaging from Narcissistic Abuse

"The first step to really, like, trying to be okay is really trying to ignore anything he says."

The Five-Step Process

1 Identify the Pattern

What to do: Notice the repeated cycle of blame, irresponsibility, and emotional manipulation

Why this matters: Pattern recognition breaks the illusion that each incident is isolated or your fault

Examples in your life:

  • He spends the last of your money on frivolous things
  • You are left to "make up for that"
  • He blames you for the financial stress
  • He refuses to take responsibility
  • The cycle repeats

The truth: This is a PATTERN, not isolated incidents

2 Reality Check

What to do: Compare your own actions and intentions with the accusations or criticisms you receive

Why this matters: Reality checking grounds you in facts, not distortions

Questions to ask yourself:

  • What did I actually do? (vs. what he says I did)
  • What were my actual intentions? (vs. what he claims they were)
  • Who is actually providing for the family?
  • Who is actually managing the household?
  • Who is actually being responsible?

The facts: YOUR actions show responsibility and care. HIS accusations are distortions.

3 Emotional Detachment

What to do: Practice not internalizing or believing the negative things your partner says

Why this matters: Emotional detachment protects your self-worth from his harmful narrative

The practice:

When he says something negative about you, remind yourself:
"This is not about me. This is his projection."

Do NOT: Take his words to heart, internalize his criticism, believe his version of you

DO: Let his words bounce off, recognize them as manipulation, hold onto your own truth

4 Reclaim Your Narrative

What to do: Remind yourself of your true character and the facts of your contributions

Why this matters: Reclaiming your narrative rebuilds your identity and counters gaslighting

Your truth:

  • I am kind and caring
  • I am responsible and hardworking
  • I provide for my family
  • I manage our household
  • I am trying my best in an impossible situation
  • I am not the problem — his behavior is

5 Strategic Response

What to do: Choose not to engage in arguments or attempts to change your partner's perspective

Why this matters: Strategic response conserves your energy and prevents further harm

Why engaging doesn't work:

  • Narcissists do not accept accountability
  • Arguments give him more ammunition to use against you
  • Trying to convince him drains your energy
  • He will twist your words and use them to hurt you

Instead: Save your energy for planning your own safety and future

Before this framework: You internalize his criticisms, doubt your worth, feel responsible for fixing everything.

After this framework (goal): You recognize the pattern, emotionally detach from his narrative, and focus on your own truth and needs.

The shift: From absorbing his harm to protecting yourself from it.

🔍 Framework #2: The "Full Picture" Perspective

"When someone shows you who they are, believe them." — Maya Angelou

The Three-Step Process

1 Zoom Out

What to do: Look at the relationship as a whole, not just isolated good or bad moments

Why this matters: Zooming out prevents being swayed by rare positive moments

The trap: One kind moment from your partner can make you forget eight harmful ones

The reality: Look at the overall pattern over weeks, months, years — not just today

2 Weigh the Evidence

What to do: Consider the ratio of positive to negative experiences

Why this matters: Weighing evidence clarifies the true nature of the relationship

The therapist's example:

If there's one kind moment and eight harmful ones, the ratio is 1:8.

That one kind moment does NOT outweigh the eight harmful ones.

The full picture shows a pattern of harm, not kindness.

Ask yourself: Over the past month, how many moments have built me up vs. torn me down?

3 Believe Patterns, Not Excuses

What to do: Trust what repeated behavior tells you, not occasional apologies or gestures

Why this matters: Believing patterns empowers you to make informed decisions based on reality

The pattern shows:

  • Irresponsibility with money
  • Lack of accountability
  • Emotional manipulation and blame
  • Leaving you to carry everything

Don't let: One apology, one nice gesture, one good day erase the consistent pattern of harm

Trust: What he DOES repeatedly, not what he SAYS occasionally

Why this framework matters: It prevents you from being manipulated by intermittent positive moments that don't reflect the true nature of the relationship.

You have to change your mindset in this situation to see the truth, and possibly do something about it.

The full picture shows: A pattern of narcissistic abuse, not a partnership of equals.

🔥 Framework #3: Strategic Use of Anger

"We can be afraid and fearful and freeze. Or we can be afraid and catapult. And run. Figure it out."

The Three-Step Process

1 Acknowledge Your Anger

What to do: Allow yourself to feel and name your anger

Why this matters: Acknowledgment validates your experience and gives you access to a powerful emotion

The truth: You SHOULD be angry. You have every right to be angry.

What anger tells you:

  • Your boundaries have been violated
  • You are being treated unfairly
  • Something needs to change
  • You deserve better

Don't suppress it. Don't minimize it. Feel it fully.

2 Channel It Productively

What to do: Use anger as motivation to make changes, not for destructive actions

Why this matters: Productive channeling turns emotion into action that serves you

Fight or flight transformed:

  • Freeze: Stay stuck in fear and inaction ❌
  • Fight (unproductive): Argue with him, try to change him ❌
  • Flight (strategic): Use anger as fuel to plan your escape and safety ✓

The goal: Transform anger into energy for change

3 Plan, Don't React

What to do: Write down your thoughts and feelings to create a clear, actionable plan

Why this matters: Planning ensures your response is effective and safe, not impulsive

Write about:

  • What specifically makes you angry
  • What you deserve instead
  • What safety looks like for you
  • What independence would mean
  • What small steps you can take now

Channel anger into: Job searching, building connections, exploring opportunities, planning for financial independence

⚠️ Important Distinction

Don't use anger to: Confront him, argue with him, try to make him see your point, seek revenge

DO use anger to: Motivate yourself, fuel your planning, propel you toward safety, energize your independence

Remember: You cannot change him. You can only change your situation.

The transformation: From powerless victim to strategic planner.

Your anger is valid. Your anger is useful. Your anger can save you.

Use it wisely.

📚 Clinical Concepts Explained

Narcissism / Narcissistic Abuse

A pattern where one person lacks empathy, refuses accountability, and manipulates others to avoid blame.

In your life: Your partner denies responsibility, projects faults onto you, and undermines your confidence

Projection

When someone attributes their own negative qualities or mistakes to another person.

In your life: Your partner blames YOU for problems HE causes (financial irresponsibility, family instability)

Gaslighting

Making someone doubt their own reality or sanity.

In your life: You are led to question your own character, worth, and decisions despite evidence of your competence and care

Fight or Flight

The body's natural response to threat — either confronting the problem or escaping it.

The therapist's guidance: Use strategic flight (planning your escape and safety) rather than freeze (staying stuck) or unproductive fight (arguing with him)

Important clarification: Your reactions are NORMAL given the circumstances.

The dysfunction comes from: Your partner's behavior, not your responses to it.

You are responding to abuse. That is different from being the problem.

📝 Your Assignment: Daily Journaling for Clarity

What to Do

Begin a daily journal. Each day, write down:

  1. Any negative comments or actions from your partner
    • What he said or did
    • The context/situation
    • Be specific and factual
  2. Your own actions and efforts for your family
    • What you did to provide or care for family
    • Responsibilities you managed
    • Problems you solved
  3. How you feel in response
    • Emotions that came up
    • Impact on your self-worth
    • Physical sensations (anxiety, tension, etc.)
  4. Any moments of anger or frustration — describe them honestly
    • What triggered the anger
    • How intense it was
    • What it's telling you

Why This Matters (Therapeutic Purpose)

This journaling helps you:

  • Separate your own reality from your partner's narrative
  • Validate your efforts and contributions
  • Identify recurring patterns of harm
  • Ground yourself in facts, not his distortions
  • Build evidence of the full picture
  • Break the cycle of gaslighting

How to Complete

Set aside 10-15 minutes each evening

Be honest and specific — This is for YOU, not for him to see

Keep it somewhere safe — Where he cannot access it

At the end of the week, review your entries and look for patterns

Bring your journal to the next session for discussion

Timeline

Start: Today

Review together: At next session

Continue: Daily, as a tool for ongoing clarity and validation

⚠️ Safety Note

Keep this journal private and secure. Do not leave it where your partner can find and read it.

Consider using a password-protected digital journal or keeping a physical journal at a trusted friend's house if necessary.
