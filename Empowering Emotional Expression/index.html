💝 Empowering Emotional Expression

Supporting Yourself and Your Child Through Effective Communication

📍 The Situation: Balancing Work Pressures and Parenting

"You can say anything to anyone in a way that they need to hear it."

What You're Navigating

At work: Pressures where compensation and recognition are tied to outcomes rather than effort

At home: Supporting your child who is processing feelings about their father

Your challenge: Wanting to help your child express themselves, but finding yourself trying to "push" your child toward certain ways of thinking or feeling

What You're Questioning

About parenting:

  • How can I help my child express their feelings?
  • Am I supposed to guide my child toward specific emotional responses?
  • Should I show vulnerability, or will that hurt my child?
  • What does "being strong" mean for my child?

About work:

  • How do I advocate for myself when outcomes are beyond my control?
  • Is my effort valued, or only results?
The common thread: In both contexts, you're grappling with how to show up authentically while navigating systems (work culture, parenting norms) that send confusing messages about value, strength, and emotional expression.

🎯 The Core Problem: Outcome-Focus vs. Process-Focus

"The output has nothing to do with you. Has a lot to do with the business."

The Pattern Identified

Your tendency: To focus on outcomes — both at work and in parenting — rather than the process or effort involved

At work this looks like:

  • Evaluating your worth based on results, not effort
  • Feeling undervalued when outcomes don't match your hard work
  • Struggling to articulate your contributions beyond deliverables

With your child this looks like:

  • Trying to guide your child toward specific emotional responses
  • Wanting your child to reach certain conclusions about their dad
  • Focusing on what you want them to understand vs. what they actually feel

The Mismatch

Your intentions: To help, to support, to be a good parent and employee

The reality:

  • Your child may need space to process and express feelings in their own way
  • Your value at work isn't solely determined by outcomes beyond your control
  • Directing emotions or pushing solutions can actually prevent authentic expression

Societal Messages You're Wrestling With

About strength and emotion: "Being strong means not showing feelings"

About parenting: "I need to protect my child from seeing me struggle"

About work: "My value is measured solely by results"

The problem: These messages create pressure to hide vulnerability, push for specific outcomes, and undervalue the process

The core issue: Focusing on outcomes rather than process creates a mismatch between your intentions (to help) and what's actually helpful (allowing space, asking questions, showing authentic emotion).

The shift needed: From directing outcomes to facilitating process — both with your child and in advocating for yourself at work.

🎁 Framework #1: The "Gift-Giving" Model of Communication

"Talking to people is like buying a gift — most people want a gift that's perfect for them."

The Three-Step Process

1 Think of Communicating as Giving a Gift

The mindset shift: Communication isn't about what YOU want to say — it's about what THEY need to hear

Just like gift-giving: The best gifts are tailored to the recipient's needs, preferences, and what matters to them

Why this matters: This approach ensures communication is empathetic, effective, and less likely to be perceived as rude or dismissive

2 Consider What You Want to Get Across AND How They'll Best Receive It

Two questions to ask yourself:

  1. What do I want to communicate? (Your message)
  2. How will this person best receive it? (Their needs)

For your child:

  • What does my child need right now? (Space, validation, questions, presence?)
  • What words/tone will feel safe to them?
  • What am I trying to get across, and is this the right time/way?

3 Use a Conflict Resolution Rubric to Structure Difficult Conversations

For difficult or important conversations: Use the structure you've previously been provided

Why this matters: Having a framework reduces anxiety and increases effectiveness

The result: Centers the recipient's experience, increasing the chance of a positive outcome

The key insight: "You can say anything to anyone in a way that they need to hear it."

This means: The message isn't the problem — how it's delivered determines whether it's received well.

Application: Instead of telling your child how to feel about their dad, consider what they need to hear: validation, curiosity, space.

❓ Framework #2: The "Ask, Don't Tell" Approach to Emotional Support

"Let him solve it for himself... just ask questions and come have a conversation."

The Three-Step Process

1 Ask Open-Ended Questions Instead of Directing or Solving

What this means: Invite your child's thoughts and feelings rather than telling them what to think or feel

Example questions:

  • "What does that mean for you?"
  • "How do you feel when that happens?"
  • "What do you miss about dad?"
  • "What's that like for you?"
  • "Tell me more about that."

Instead of: "You should feel..." or "Dad is..." or "Here's what you need to understand..."

2 Reflect Back What You Notice

What this means: Mirror what you observe without judgment or interpretation

Example phrases:

  • "I noticed you seemed sad when..."
  • "It sounds like you're feeling..."
  • "I see that this is hard for you."

Why this works: Reflection validates their experience without telling them what they should feel

3 Allow Your Child to Come Up With Their Own Solutions or Theories

What this means: Resist the urge to fix, solve, or direct — let them lead

The therapist's guidance: "Continue to ask questions... you'll learn a lot more about your child"

Why this matters:

  • Fosters autonomy and self-understanding
  • Builds trust between you and your child
  • Prevents you from imposing your own narrative
  • Ensures your child's ACTUAL concerns are addressed, not what you assume

✓ The Power of This Approach

What you avoid: Pushing your child toward specific emotional responses they're not ready for

What you gain: Your child develops their own understanding, emotional vocabulary, and trust in you

Example transformation:

Before (Telling): "You need to understand that your dad loves you, he just has problems."

After (Asking): "What do you miss about dad? How do you feel when he doesn't call?"

💫 Framework #3: Modeling Emotional Expression

"Being strong is just most of the time having the courage or effort to just kind of bear with it or talk about it. Showing the emotion or having him just kinda talk about it. That's strong."

The Three-Step Process

1 Show Your Own Feelings in Age-Appropriate, Responsible Ways

What this means: Don't hide all your emotions from your child

Age-appropriate examples:

  • Letting your child see you're sad sometimes
  • Crying when something is difficult (not hiding in another room)
  • Expressing frustration in healthy ways
  • Showing joy and excitement openly

Responsible means: Not overwhelming them with adult problems, but showing emotions exist and are manageable

2 Talk Through Your Emotions With Your Child

What this means: Explain what you feel and why, making emotions less mysterious

Example phrases:

  • "I feel sad sometimes too, and that's okay"
  • "I'm frustrated because [simple explanation], but I'll be alright"
  • "This makes me happy because..."

Why this works: Demystifies emotions and shows they're normal, temporary, and manageable

3 Normalize Emotional Experiences

What this means: Demonstrate that it's okay to feel and express a range of emotions

The critical insight: "If mom's always strong and she never cries, maybe that is how I should be"

What your child learns when you hide emotions:

  • Emotions are bad or shameful
  • I should suppress my feelings
  • Strength means never showing vulnerability
  • I have to handle everything alone

What your child learns when you model healthy expression:

  • Emotions are normal
  • It's safe to feel and express feelings
  • Strength includes courage to be vulnerable
  • We can talk about hard things

⚠️ Important Truth

"Hiding things from kids is never the best thing to do. Kids pick up on small things."

What this means: Even when you try to hide emotions, your child senses something is wrong

The result: They feel confused, anxious, or think they're the problem

Better approach: Show appropriate emotion and explain it simply

Why modeling matters: Children learn how to process and express emotions by observing adults.

When you hide emotions: You teach children to suppress their own.

When you show healthy expression: You model healthy coping and normalize the full range of human emotion.

Redefining strength: "Being strong is just most of the time having the courage or effort to just kind of bear with it or talk about it."

📊 Framework #4: Outcome vs. Effort (Work Context)

"Look up those terms... so that you can have the language when you actually go in and have the meeting."

The Three-Step Process

1 Distinguish Between Outcome-Focused Pay and Effort/Resource-Driven Work

Outcome-focused pay: Compensation based on results (sales targets, deliverables, metrics)

Effort/resource-driven work: Value based on input (hours, expertise, workload, process)

The problem: When you're paid for outcomes but outcomes depend on factors beyond your control (market conditions, timing, other departments)

2 Recognize That Your Value Is Not Solely Determined by Outcomes

The therapist's insight: "The output has nothing to do with you. Has a lot to do with the business"

What this means:

  • Results often depend on external factors
  • Your effort, expertise, and process have inherent value
  • Hard work matters even when outcomes don't align

3 Use This Language When Discussing Compensation or Performance

Preparation needed: Research and write down definitions for these terms

In meetings, you can say:

  • "I understand compensation is tied to outcomes, but I want to highlight the effort and resources I've invested..."
  • "While results were affected by [external factors], my workload and expertise remained consistent..."
  • "I'd like to discuss how we can better recognize effort alongside outcomes..."
Why this framework matters: It reframes self-worth and advocacy at work, helping you articulate your needs and contributions more effectively.

The shift: From "I didn't achieve the outcome, so I'm not valuable" to "My effort and expertise matter regardless of external factors affecting outcomes."

🛠️ Practical Instructions: What to Do

With Your Child

✓ Five Key Practices

  1. When your child expresses a feeling, reflect back what you notice
    • "I noticed you seemed sad when..."
  2. Ask open-ended questions
    • "What does that mean for you?"
    • "How do you feel about that?"
  3. Avoid jumping in with solutions or interpretations
    • Let your child lead the conversation
  4. Share your own feelings in a calm, age-appropriate way
    • "I feel sad sometimes too, and that's okay"
  5. Use the "gift-giving" mindset
    • Tailor your words to what your child needs, not just what you want to say

At Work

✓ Three Key Actions

  1. Research the terms
    • "Outcome-focused pay"
    • "Effort/resource-driven work"
  2. Prepare language for meetings
    • Highlight your effort and workload, not just outcomes
    • Connect external factors to results
  3. If discussing ADHD with your boss
    • Share your experience
    • Offer support in a non-judgmental way

What to Avoid

⚠️ Don't Do These Things

  • With your child: Avoid telling them how they "should" feel or what they "should" do
  • With yourself: Avoid hiding your emotions entirely — model healthy expression instead
  • At work: Avoid framing your value solely in terms of outcomes

In your life: Asking your child questions instead of telling them what to feel about their dad
