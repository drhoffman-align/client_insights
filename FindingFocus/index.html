💊 Framework #2: Medication Education

"Think of the long-acting stimulant like a dimmer switch, not a light switch — it comes on gradually."

Understanding Your Medication Options

Types of Stimulants: Short-Acting vs. Long-Acting

Short-acting (Immediate Release):

  • Quick onset — works within 30-60 minutes
  • Wears off faster — lasts 3-4 hours
  • Like a "light switch" — on and off effect
  • May cause more GI upset

Long-acting (Extended Release / XR):

  • Gradual onset — builds up over time
  • Lasts 8-12 hours
  • Like a "dimmer switch" — smooth, gradual effect
  • Less likely to cause GI upset
  • This is what you'll be starting with

How Stimulants Work

In the brain: Increase dopamine and norepinephrine levels

The result: Improved focus, attention, and ability to complete tasks

The connection to caffeine: Stimulant medications work similarly to how caffeine was helping you "lock in" — but more consistently and without the crashes

Side Effects to Monitor

Common side effects (usually manageable):

  • Dry mouth — Drink plenty of water
  • Decreased appetite — Keep regular eating intervals
  • Headaches — Often improve with hydration
  • GI effects — Especially in the morning (less common with extended release)
  • Possible jitteriness — Usually improves as body adjusts

Why this matters: Knowing side effects and safety reduces anxiety and stigma

Safety and Addiction

The truth about addiction: "Most people don't get addicted to these medications at prescribed doses"

Why stimulants are controlled: They CAN be misused, but this is rare when taken as prescribed

Higher risk if: History of substance abuse (not your situation)

At prescribed doses: Minimal addiction risk

Monitoring and Adjustment

The approach: Start low, go slow, and adjust as needed

What you'll track:

  • Focus and concentration
  • Energy levels
  • Appetite
  • Sleep quality
  • Any side effects
  • Work performance
  • Memory improvements
  • Relationship impacts

Why this matters: Ongoing monitoring ensures the best fit for you individually

Understanding options empowers informed choices. You're part of this decision-making process.

Knowing side effects and safety reduces anxiety. These medications are well-studied and safe when used as prescribed.

Ongoing monitoring ensures the best fit. This is collaborative — your feedback guides adjustments.

💊 Your Medication Plan

Adderall XR (Extended Release) 10mg

Dosage & Timing: One capsule once daily in the morning

Purpose: To improve focus, attention, and reduce distractibility associated with ADHD

How to take:

  • Take one capsule in the morning with water
  • Do NOT crush or open the capsule (this would destroy the extended-release mechanism)
  • Take early in the day to avoid sleep disruption
  • "Take the medication early in the day so it doesn't disrupt your sleep"

Expected benefits:

  • Improved ability to focus
  • Better task completion
  • Less reliance on caffeine
  • Smoother transitions between tasks
  • Reduced daily friction

Side effects to monitor:

  • Dry mouth → Drink plenty of water
  • Decreased appetite → Keep regular eating intervals
  • Headaches → Hydration often helps
  • GI upset (especially in morning) → Usually less with XR
  • Possible jitteriness → Often improves with time

Safety warnings:

  • Do NOT take late in the day (may cause insomnia)
  • Do NOT increase dose without consulting therapist
  • Report any severe side effects: chest pain, severe anxiety, significant mood changes

Rationale: Long-acting stimulant provides steady focus with fewer peaks and crashes; less likely to cause GI upset than short-acting forms

Adjustment: Dose may be adjusted after 2-3 months based on response and side effects

Supporting Your Medication Success

Hydration: Drink plenty of water to help with dry mouth and headaches

Nutrition: Maintain regular eating intervals to avoid appetite suppression issues

Sleep hygiene: Take medication early; maintain good sleep routine

Communication: Report any side effects or concerns promptly

📚 Clinical Concepts Explained

ADHD (Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder)

A neurodevelopmental condition marked by inattention, distractibility, impulsivity, and sometimes hyperactivity.

In adults: Often shows up as trouble focusing, forgetfulness, and difficulty switching tasks

Hyperfocus

The tendency to become so absorbed in one task that other things are ignored — common in ADHD.

Your experience: "One track mind" where you miss buildings, details, conversations

Stimulant Medications

Drugs like Adderall and Ritalin that increase dopamine and norepinephrine in the brain, improving focus and attention.

Extended Release vs. Immediate Release

Extended release (long-acting): Provides a gradual effect ("dimmer switch")

Immediate release (short-acting): Acts quickly but wears off sooner ("light switch")

Addiction Risk

At prescribed doses, stimulants are rarely addictive.

Misuse is more likely in: Those with a history of substance abuse (not your situation)
