💬 Breaking the Loop

Building Consideration and Communication in Relationships

📍 The Situation: Caught in a Communication Cycle

"It tends to be sort of a closed loop... you wanna talk about something or you have feelings about something. And there's a response or reaction which is pretty closed. And then you become more guarded."

What You're Experiencing

The pattern: Navigating a difficult period in your marriage, marked by repeated cycles of miscommunication and emotional disconnect with your partner

What it feels like: Your efforts to communicate and be considerate are not reciprocated, leading to frustration, sadness, and a sense of being unappreciated

Recent examples: Proposing ideas (such as a tour) and sharing thoughtful written communication, only to have your partner dismiss or not engage with your input

The emotional toll: Compounded by grief over impending changes (your partner leaving) and the struggle to balance your own needs with those of your partner

The core problem: A persistent feedback loop of failed communication and lack of mutual consideration.

What this looks like: You feel you are the only one initiating meaningful conversations, while your partner remains closed off, reactive, and unwilling to engage or consider your perspective.

The result: You feel unheard and undervalued, leading you to withdraw or shut down emotionally.

The mismatch: Between your intention to be considerate and your partner's perception, as well as a lack of shared language and understanding about what "consideration" truly means in your relationship.

🔍 Understanding Key Concepts

Grief

What it is: Not just about loss, but also about change and unmet expectations

For you: Your grief is multifaceted, involving both sadness over your partner leaving and frustration about the state of your relationship

"Grief can involve multiple, sometimes conflicting, emotions; it's normal to feel both sadness and gratitude."

Feedback Loop

What it is: A repeating cycle where one person's actions (or inactions) reinforce the other's, leading to a stuck pattern

In your case: Your attempts to communicate are met with disengagement, causing you to withdraw, which then reinforces your partner's perception that you are not listening or open

Consideration

The definition: Being thoughtful, kind, and mindful of another's feelings, rights, and needs

The therapist's observation: Your actions meet this standard, even if your partner does not recognize it

"Considerate: describe someone kind, and mindful of others' feelings, rights, and needs."

Engagement vs. Reaction

Engagement: Involves curiosity and thoughtful response

Reaction: Immediate and often defensive

Why this distinction matters: Helps you see where communication breaks down

"When there's no engagement, there's no understanding that happens."

🎯 Framework #1: Emotional Differentiation and Action

"Picking those [emotions] apart is helpful in sort of distinguishing or figuring out what to do about it."

The Four-Step Process

1 Identify and Name the Emotions Present

Examples: Sadness, anxiety, grief

2 Explore the "Why" Behind Each Emotion

The question: What is driving this feeling?

3 Determine What Action the Emotion Is Calling For

Examples: Rest for sadness, action for anxiety

"Actions, you know, they're usually for you to do something — whether it is, you know, depression, whether it might be rest or something like that, or, you know, where you... it's energy depleting."

4 Reflect on Whether the Action Is for Self-Care or Relationship Repair

The purpose: Understanding what you truly need

Why this framework matters:

Understanding the specific emotions and their causes helps prevent acting on generalized distress and enables more targeted, effective responses

For you: You experience multiple emotions (grief, frustration, sadness) around your partner's departure and your communication struggles. By naming these emotions and exploring their origins, you can better understand what you need (e.g., support, validation, rest).

"Emotional clarity is the first step to effective action — name your feelings and understand their origins."

🔄 Framework #2: The Communication Loop Model

"It tends to be sort of a closed loop... you wanna talk about something or you have feelings about something. And there's a response or reaction which is pretty closed. And then you become more guarded."

The Four-Step Process

1 Recognize the Pattern

The cycle: One partner initiates, the other shuts down, leading to disengagement and repetition of the cycle

2 Identify Points Where Engagement or Curiosity Is Missing

What to look for: Where does the conversation close down?

3 Introduce or Request Engagement

Examples: Asking questions, showing curiosity

4 Reflect on the Outcome and Adjust Approach as Needed

The learning: What worked? What didn't?

Why this framework matters:

Breaking the closed-loop pattern is essential for mutual understanding and emotional connection

For you: The therapist highlighted how you often initiate conversations or bring up issues, but your partner's lack of engagement leads to a cycle where you feel unheard and eventually withdraw.

Example: When you sent a thoughtful email, your partner did not read it, and when you read it aloud, he responded without real engagement.

"Communication breakdowns often follow a predictable loop — recognizing the pattern is key to changing it."

💝 Framework #3: Consideration Definition and Application

"I think it's pretty important to be considered, and that is simple — just being thought about every now and then."

The Four-Step Process

1 Define "Consideration" Using a Shared, Objective Standard

The definition: Thoughtful, kind, mindful of others' feelings, rights, and needs

"Considerate: describe someone kind, and mindful of others' feelings, rights, and needs."

2 Assess Your Own Actions Against This Definition

The question: Am I being considerate?

3 Assess Your Partner's Actions Similarly

The question: Is my partner being considerate?

4 Use This Shared Language to Discuss and Negotiate Needs

The benefit: Reduces subjectivity and defensiveness

"Having a common language... this is where we work from. And this is where we establish ourselves at the center of this."

Why this framework matters:

Having a common definition reduces subjectivity and defensiveness, making it easier to address relational needs constructively

For you: You are consistently thoughtful about your partner's feelings and needs, often at the expense of your own. The therapist used the definition of consideration to validate your efforts and to highlight the lack of reciprocity from your partner.

"You tend to be very considerate even in your conversations with me, it's a lot of what he thinks and what he might say."

The encouragement: Reflect on whether you feel considered and use this as a measure for healthy relationships.

🛠️ Practical Instructions

When Feeling Strong Emotions

Pause to identify and name them before acting

Ask Yourself

Why do I feel this way and what do I need in the moment?

When Communicating with Your Partner

Clearly state your intentions and invite his input, but also notice if he is engaging or simply reacting

Use the Shared Definition of Consideration

To assess both your own and your partner's actions

If You Feel Unheard

Calmly point out specific examples (e.g., "I sent you a letter because I cared about this, and I noticed you didn't read it")

"My goal is not to make you feel away negatively about your partner, but I also think that there are things that you could say or do to really, in the moment, sort of say what's happening."

Avoid Internalizing Blame

For your partner's lack of engagement; recognize what is and isn't within your control

"Lack of engagement from your partner is not your fault; it reflects his own limitations or choices."

Use the Feelings Wheel

To help articulate your emotions when reflecting or journaling
