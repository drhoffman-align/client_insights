📦 Framework #1: Room-by-Room Organizational System

"The trash can is, like, the best storage unit."

The Five-Step Process

1 Label and Section Items by CATEGORY (Not by Room)

Start in one room: Pick any room to begin

Create categories: Books, office supplies, clothes, kitchen items, electronics, sentimental items, etc.

NOT by destination room: Don't think "bedroom pile" — think "books pile" regardless of where they'll go

Why this works: Categories are clearer than room destinations when you're overwhelmed

2 Create Piles for Items That Belong in Other Rooms

Make piles for:

  • Items that belong in kitchen
  • Items that belong in bedroom
  • Items that belong in bathroom
  • Items that belong in living areas
  • TRASH pile (critical!)

Therapist's wisdom: "The trash can is, like, the best storage unit."

3 Do NOT Leave the Room Until Everything Is Separated

Finish the room: Don't move on until EVERYTHING in that room is sorted into a pile

Why this matters: Prevents feeling scattered, creates clear completion point

Creates visible progress: When room is fully sorted, you can SEE what you've accomplished

Reduces overwhelm: One room at a time = manageable chunks

4 Move Piles to Their Respective Locations and Repeat

Transport piles: Take each category pile to where it belongs

Repeat the process: In the next room, sort everything again by category

Build momentum: Each completed room makes the next one easier

5 Include a TRASH Pile and Be Decisive

Make a trash pile in EVERY room: This is non-negotiable

Be decisive about discarding: When in doubt, throw it out (with exceptions for sentimental items)

Pro tip for your situation: "Hide the trash pile from your mother to avoid conflict"

Move trash boxes near exit: Get them out of the house as soon as possible

Why This Framework Works

Prevents overwhelm: Breaking into room-by-room, pile-by-pile creates manageable steps instead of impossible mountain.

Creates visible progress: Each completed room is tangible achievement you can see and feel.

Reduces decision fatigue: Clear system means fewer decisions to make when you're already exhausted.

Builds momentum: Completing first room makes starting second room easier.

✂️ Framework #2: Needs-Based Decluttering

"If you don't know it's here, it probably don't need to be here."

The Three-Step Process

1 List What Is ABSOLUTELY NEEDED to Function

For each room in NEW space, ask: What do I absolutely NEED to function here?

Kitchen example:

  • 1-2 pots, 1-2 pans
  • One set of dishes for yourself
  • Essential utensils (fork, knife, spoon, spatula)
  • Can opener, cutting board, knife

Bedroom example:

  • 2-3 weeks of clothes maximum
  • Bedding
  • Alarm clock/phone charger

2 Identify and Set Aside ONLY Those Items

Be ruthless: If it's not on your "absolute needs" list, it goes in a different pile

Set aside essentials: These are what you pack FIRST and carefully

Everything else: Gets evaluated with the two-year rule

3 Discard or Donate Everything Else

The two-year rule: If you haven't used it in two years and it's not sentimental, toss it

Can't find it?: "If you don't know it's here, it probably don't need to be here"

For clothes: Keep 2-3 weeks worth; donate or discard the rest

Therapist's encouragement: "Throwing it away is much easier" than moving it

You can always get more: If you truly need something later, you can replace it

Why This Framework Works

Focuses energy on essentials: Given tight deadline and limited energy, can't waste effort on unnecessary items.

Reduces clutter: Moving less = easier move = less to unpack = simpler life on other side.

Simplifies the move: Fewer decisions, fewer boxes, less time spent packing.

Transformational opportunity: Therapist frames this as "getting rid of old shit and old you" — chance to start fresh.

🧠 Framework #3: Intentional Thinking & Cognitive Restructuring

"The answer is intention."

The Four-Step Process

1 Notice Negative or Self-Defeating Thoughts

Pay attention when you think:

  • "I can't do this"
  • "This is impossible"
  • "I'm not capable"
  • "Nothing ever works out for me"
  • "Why even try"

These are the thoughts that keep you stuck

2 Consciously Choose Whether to Believe or Challenge

Therapist's key insight: "You get a choice — option to believe it or not."

The truth: These thoughts FEEL real because you keep believing them

But: Feeling real ≠ being true

You have agency: You can CHOOSE not to believe the negative thought

Challenge it: "Is this actually true? What evidence contradicts this? What have I successfully done in the past?"

3 Practice "Fake It Till You Make It"

Act AS IF you believe in your capability: Even if you don't feel it yet

Therapist's guidance: "Believing in yourself has all sorts of benefits. Long term."

What this looks like:

  • Take action as if you're capable (even when you feel you're not)
  • Speak to yourself as if you believe in yourself
  • Make decisions from place of capability, not incapacity

The mechanism: Acting as if eventually changes how you feel — behavior shapes belief

4 Regularly Reflect on Your Thinking in New Environment

Therapist's recommendation: Go to a coffee shop to reflect on your thoughts

Why new environment matters: Physical change of space helps shift mental patterns

What to do there:

  • Think about what you're thinking about
  • Identify patterns in your negative thoughts
  • Challenge how wrong those thoughts are
  • Review documents therapist sent
  • Journal or take notes

Key quote: "You have to consistently find some sort of time to think about what you're thinking about — and how wrong it is."

Why This Framework Works

Repeated negative thinking reinforces helplessness: Every time you believe "I can't," you make it more true in your experience.

Intentional thought challenges break the cycle: Consciously choosing NOT to believe negative thoughts disrupts the reinforcement loop.

Builds self-efficacy: Acting AS IF you're capable, even when you don't feel it, eventually creates actual capability and confidence.

The answer is intention: Making conscious choices about thoughts and actions, rather than operating on autopilot of negativity.

⏰ Framework #4: Routine & Medication Adherence

"Take your medicine. It will get you out of bed."

The Three-Step Process

1 Take Adderall at Same Time Every Morning

Set time: 8:00 AM every day

REGARDLESS of mood: Don't wait until you "feel like it"

Don't negotiate: Not up for debate based on how you feel that morning

Set alarm: Make it non-negotiable part of your routine

Therapist's directive: "Take your medicine. It will get you out of bed."

2 Use the Medication's Activation to Get Out of Bed

As SOON as you take Adderall: Get out of bed

Don't lie back down: Even if you want to

Start your day immediately: Brush teeth, get dressed, eat something

Use medication's energy: Let it propel you into action rather than lying in bed waiting to "feel motivated"

3 Establish Autopilot Routines for Sleep and Meals

Eat at regular times: Even if you don't feel hungry

Sleep at consistent time: Set bedtime routine

Make these autopilot: Require minimal decision-making

Why this matters: Frees up mental energy for more important tasks (like packing, dealing with emotions, managing move)

Why This Framework Works

Consistency supports functioning: Medication and routines for sleep/meals create foundation for energy and motivation.

Removes negotiation: When you don't have to decide "should I take medication today?" or "should I eat?", you conserve willpower for harder decisions.

Breaks avoidance pattern: Tendency to stay in bed and delay action gets interrupted by mandatory medication timing.

Creates momentum: Once up and medication kicks in, initiating other tasks becomes significantly easier.
